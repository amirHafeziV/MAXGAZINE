Gold Plunges Below $4,000 as Dollar Strengthens on September Rate Hike Bets

Gold fell more than 3% to $4,016 per ounce on June 24 — its lowest since November 2025 — as a stronger U.S. dollar and rising expectations of a September Federal Reserve rate hike pulled capital away from non-yielding safe-haven assets.

Published Elements Desk · 2026-06-24
AD SPACEIn-article bannerBook this spot →

Gold prices fell sharply on June 24, 2026, dropping more than 3% to $4,016 per troy ounce — the lowest level since November 2025 — as a combination of U.S. dollar strength and rising Federal Reserve rate expectations pressured the safe-haven metal.

The decline marks a significant reversal from the commodity's 2026 trajectory. Earlier in the year, gold had been on a sustained upward trend, driven by geopolitical uncertainty, central bank purchases from emerging market economies, and expectations of a dovish Fed pivot. Analysts at the World Bank had projected commodity prices rising for the first time since 2022, led by energy and gold demand.

What Changed

The reversal traces to shifting expectations around the Federal Reserve. Market participants now assign approximately a 68% probability to a September 2026 rate hike — up sharply from 29% just one week earlier. The shift was driven by U.S. economic data released in the week of June 24: the services PMI rose to 51.3, the manufacturing PMI climbed to 55.7, and the composite PMI reached 52.2. Taken together, the data suggests the U.S. economy continues to expand at a pace inconsistent with imminent rate cuts.

The Dollar Index (DXY) responded by strengthening, making gold more expensive in non-dollar currencies and reducing its appeal for global buyers. EUR/USD slipped to around 1.1380 from the high end of its recent trading range.

A stronger dollar and higher real rates are the two most reliable suppressors of gold demand, and both are moving in the same direction simultaneously as of late June 2026.

Context: The 2026 Commodity Narrative Reverses

Gold had reached its record above $4,000 earlier in the year on the back of aggressive central bank buying — primarily from China, India, Turkey, and Eastern European economies diversifying away from dollar reserves — and from elevated geopolitical risk premiums following regional conflicts in early 2026.

The June 24 drop does not erase that structural demand, which analysts note remains intact beneath the surface. But it represents a reminder that gold's price is also highly sensitive to short-term interest rate expectations, even when structural demand is strong.

EUR/USD and the Broader FX Picture

EUR/USD held around 1.1380 on June 24, under pressure from the same forces hitting gold: stronger U.S. data and hawkish Fed repricing. Deutsche Bank has a year-end target of 1.2500 and Morgan Stanley targets 1.3000 — implying significant dollar weakness ahead if those calls prove correct — but the near-term direction has shifted decisively in favor of the dollar.

What to Watch

The September 27, 2026 Federal Reserve meeting is now the critical event on the calendar for gold traders. If the Fed holds rates or signals a pivot, gold is likely to recover quickly. If Warsh proceeds with the hike, the pressure on gold could extend further toward the $3,800–3,900 range that technical analysts have flagged as the next support zone.
