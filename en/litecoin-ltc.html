About

Litecoin (LTC) is one of the oldest cryptocurrencies still in active use, created in 2011 as a lighter, faster complement to Bitcoin. It was built from Bitcoin's open-source code but changed several key parameters, and it is often described as "digital silver" to Bitcoin's "digital gold" — a coin designed primarily for everyday payments rather than long-term store of value alone.

Like Bitcoin, Litecoin runs on a public, decentralized blockchain secured by proof-of-work mining. The most important technical difference is the hashing algorithm: instead of Bitcoin's SHA-256, Litecoin uses Scrypt, which was originally chosen to be more memory-intensive and, in the early days, more accessible to ordinary hardware. Litecoin also produces a new block roughly every 2.5 minutes — four times faster than Bitcoin's ten-minute target — so transactions are confirmed more quickly, which suits point-of-sale and remittance use cases.

The supply schedule mirrors Bitcoin's design at a larger scale. Litecoin's maximum supply is capped at 84 million coins, exactly four times Bitcoin's 21 million. New LTC enters circulation as a block reward paid to miners, and like Bitcoin that reward is cut in half roughly every four years in an event called the halving, gradually slowing the creation of new coins.

Key use cases

Litecoin's main role has always been fast, low-cost transfers of value. Because fees are typically very small and confirmations are quick, it is widely supported by exchanges, payment processors and crypto debit cards, and it is often used as a low-friction way to move funds between platforms.

  • Payments and remittances: quick settlement and low fees make LTC practical for spending and cross-border transfers.
  • A testing ground for upgrades: Litecoin has historically adopted technical improvements early, which the wider community then watched closely.
  • Optional privacy: the MimbleWimble Extension Blocks (MWEB) upgrade lets users send more confidential transactions when they choose to.

Litecoin is intentionally conservative in its design philosophy. Rather than trying to be a platform for complex applications, it has focused for over a decade on doing one thing reliably — moving money quickly and cheaply — while remaining one of the most liquid and widely listed cryptocurrencies on the market.

History

Litecoin was created by Charlie Lee, a former Google engineer who later worked at the cryptocurrency exchange Coinbase. He announced the project on a Bitcoin forum in early October 2011, and the network went live on October 13, 2011. Lee's goal was not to replace Bitcoin but to create a complementary network that was faster and cheaper for smaller, everyday payments, taking Bitcoin's proven design and tuning it for speed.

The two defining choices made at launch — the Scrypt hashing algorithm and the 2.5-minute block time — set Litecoin apart from the start. In its early years it grew alongside Bitcoin, becoming one of the first "altcoins" to gain real liquidity and exchange support, and it has remained a fixture of the top cryptocurrencies ever since.

Major milestones

Litecoin built a reputation as an early adopter of important technical upgrades. In May 2017 it activated Segregated Witness (SegWit) ahead of Bitcoin, and shortly afterward it was used for some of the first Lightning Network transactions, demonstrating fast off-chain payments. This pattern of moving early made Litecoin a kind of proving ground that the broader Bitcoin community watched closely.

In December 2017, during a major market peak, Charlie Lee disclosed that he had sold and donated nearly all of his personal LTC holdings, saying he wanted to avoid any conflict of interest when commenting on the coin's price. The move was unusual for a project founder and drew both criticism and praise.

A significant technical addition came in May 2022, when Litecoin activated MimbleWimble Extension Blocks (MWEB). This optional feature lets users send confidential transactions that hide amounts, improving privacy and fungibility for those who opt in, while keeping the main chain transparent for everyone else.

Throughout its history, Litecoin's price has moved through the same broad boom-and-bust cycles as the rest of the market, often rising and falling in step with Bitcoin. Its halvings — which have reduced the block reward over time — have been closely followed as supply milestones, and the Litecoin Foundation, a non-profit established to support the project, has continued to coordinate development, education and partnerships over the years. More than a decade after launch, Litecoin remains one of the most recognizable and consistently traded names in the industry.

Recent Developments

More than a decade after its launch, Litecoin is trying to broaden from a pure payments coin into something more programmable, while keeping its identity as fast, low-cost money. The most notable effort is LitVM, an EVM-compatible layer-2 project announced to bring smart contracts and decentralized applications to the Litecoin ecosystem. Testnets for this rollup went live and processed large volumes of transactions, signaling an attempt to keep Litecoin relevant as DeFi and on-chain applications grow elsewhere.

The MimbleWimble privacy feature has also been back in focus. In 2026 the network dealt with security issues connected to MWEB and shipped a series of emergency patches to harden the code, an episode that underlined both the challenges of maintaining optional-privacy systems and the responsiveness of Litecoin's development community. Reporting on these patches stressed that the network continued operating and that fixes were released promptly.

Institutional and market positioning

On the regulated-products side, spot Litecoin exchange-traded funds (ETFs) have moved forward in the United States, with at least one spot LTC product reaching the market — a milestone that, as with Bitcoin and Ethereum, gives traditional investors a way to gain exposure to Litecoin through regulated brokerage accounts. In Europe, banks operating under the MiCA framework have begun offering Litecoin alongside Bitcoin to retail customers, widening access through established financial channels.

These developments place Litecoin in an interesting position. It is simultaneously one of the oldest and most conservative cryptocurrencies — valued for reliability, deep liquidity and broad merchant and exchange support — and a network now experimenting with smart-contract layers and privacy upgrades. Its long track record as a payments-focused coin, combined with growing access through ETFs and regulated banks, keeps it firmly among the most widely held digital assets, even as the broader market remains volatile. As always, none of this constitutes a prediction about future price, and Litecoin trades within the same uncertain, cyclical market as the rest of the sector.
