German Court Rules Google Liable for Its AI-Generated Search Answers

A Munich regional court stripped Google of its search-intermediary safe harbor and held it directly liable for false claims made by AI Overviews — a landmark ruling that could reshape AI content liability across Europe.

The Regional Court of Munich issued a ruling on June 9, 2026 that could fundamentally alter the legal landscape for AI-powered search. In case no. 26 O 869/26, the court found Google directly liable for false statements generated by its AI Overviews feature — rejecting the company's argument that it functions merely as a search intermediary and is therefore shielded from liability for content it surfaces.

The case was brought by two Munich-based publishers whose businesses had been falsely linked by Google's AI Overviews to scams, subscription traps, and dubious commercial practices. According to the court, the AI system had mixed up information about genuinely problematic third parties with the plaintiffs, drawing connections that did not appear in any of the sources the feature cited. The court issued a preliminary injunction barring Google from repeating the false claims.

The Legal Theory: AI Author, Not Search Conduit

The ruling rests on a meaningful distinction. Traditional search engines have long been treated as conduits — platforms that index and surface third-party content without substantial editorial intervention, qualifying them for intermediary liability protections similar to those afforded to telecommunications providers. Courts and regulators in most jurisdictions have accepted this framing.

The Munich court rejected it for AI Overviews. Because the feature synthesizes information and generates new content rather than simply listing source URLs, the court concluded that Google is acting as an author. An author bears responsibility for what they write — and cannot point to third-party sources as a shield when the final output is a new, independently generated statement.

The ruling is one of the first in Europe to apply this logic to a live generative AI product deployed at consumer scale.

Implications for AI Search Products

AI Overviews and comparable features — including Microsoft Copilot in Bing, Perplexity's answer engine, and similar tools — have become central to how hundreds of millions of users encounter information online. If the Munich ruling is followed by other courts or codified by legislators, it would impose a new layer of editorial and legal accountability on companies that generate answers, not just list sources.

For Google specifically, the decision arrives as AI Overviews is already the subject of criticism over factual accuracy. The company has repeatedly updated the feature since its public launch following reports of hallucinations and misleading outputs. A sustained liability exposure in Europe could accelerate internal changes to how AI Overviews is designed, filtered, and audited before serving answers.

What Comes Next

The Munich ruling is a preliminary injunction from a regional court — it does not automatically set binding precedent across Germany or the EU. Google has options to appeal. However, it establishes a legal theory that plaintiffs in similar situations can now invoke, and it may encourage other European courts to follow the same reasoning.

Legal analysts also note that the ruling arrives as the EU AI Act enters its enforcement phases. The Act does not directly address search liability, but the Munich court's logic — that generating new content means accepting authorial responsibility — aligns with the broader regulatory direction in Europe toward treating AI systems as active agents rather than passive tools.
