US Bans Federal Reserve From Issuing CBDC Until 2030 — Trump Signs Today

Congress passed a 358-32 housing bill banning the Federal Reserve from issuing any CBDC through end-2030, while carving out private stablecoins like USDC and Tether.

Published MAXGAZINE SEO Desk · 2026-06-25
AD SPACEIn-article bannerBook this spot →

The US House of Representatives voted 358-32 on Tuesday to approve a landmark housing bill that prohibits the Federal Reserve from issuing a central bank digital currency (CBDC) until end-2030. The US Senate had passed the bill 85-5 on Monday. President Donald Trump is expected to sign the legislation into law Wednesday, delivering a long-sought Republican victory against centrally controlled digital currencies.

Congress Unites Behind Historic Vote

The 21st Century ROAD to Housing Act was designed primarily to address housing affordability, but a single clause banning CBDCs has made it one of the most consequential pieces of legislation for the crypto industry in years. The bipartisan margin — 358-32 in the House, 85-5 in the Senate — reflects unusual cross-aisle consensus.

Under the bill, the Federal Reserve may not "directly or indirectly issue or create a central bank digital currency or any digital asset that is substantially similar to a central bank digital currency" through December 31, 2030. Senate Banking Committee Chairman Tim Scott called it "a major win for families working toward the American Dream."

Stablecoins Carved Out

The legislation explicitly exempts stablecoins, permitting "dollar-denominated currency that is open, permissionless and private" — a description that covers assets like USDC and Tether. This distinction reflects a key principle: Washington will tolerate private digital dollars while blocking a government-run version.

Privacy advocates have long warned that a Fed-issued CBDC would enable granular surveillance of financial transactions. The carve-out signals that Congress is drawing a clear line between permissioned government money and open, decentralized finance.

Political Roots: Years in the Making

Representative Tom Emmer's Anti-CBDC Surveillance State Act passed the House in July 2025 but stalled in the Senate. Attaching the ban to the housing bill — which enjoyed far broader support — proved the winning strategy. Trump, who has consistently opposed CBDCs as instruments of "financial control," is expected to sign the bill on the same day it reaches his desk.

Attention now shifts to the CLARITY Act, the Senate's crypto market structure bill. Galaxy Digital estimates a 60% chance of passage before year-end, with the August recess and November midterms compressing the calendar.

Conclusion: A Strategic Win With an Expiry Date

The US CBDC ban through 2030 is a meaningful but temporary victory for the crypto industry. The prohibition expires at year-end 2030, giving Congress roughly four years before a future administration could restart the conversation. In the meantime, private stablecoins gain room to expand, Bitcoin's institutional credentials strengthen, and US crypto policy 2026 acquires greater clarity. The more consequential question may be what happens after 2030 — and whether the line Washington has now drawn will hold.
