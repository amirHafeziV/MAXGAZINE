FIFA Uses Avalanche Blockchain to Fight World Cup 2026 Ticket Scalping

FIFA's custom Avalanche Layer-1 chain has processed over 60,000 blockchain ticket transactions for the 2026 World Cup, generating more than $25 million in volume as a Right-to-Buy token model shifts control of the secondary market away from scalpers.

As the 2026 FIFA World Cup unfolds across stadiums in the United States, Canada, and Mexico, a quiet technological experiment is reshaping how the world's most sought-after event tickets change hands. FIFA has deployed a custom Layer-1 blockchain built on Avalanche to manage a portion of its ticketing inventory, targeting the perennial problem of scalping and fraud that has plagued major sporting events for decades.

The FIFA Collect platform, which handles digital collectibles and fan engagement, uses the Avalanche-based chain — known internally as the FIFA Blockchain — to issue two distinct digital entitlements: a Right-to-Buy (RTB) and a Right-to-Ticket (RTT). An RTB gives verified fans priority access to purchase a specific ticket before general availability, while an RTT represents the ticket itself once purchased. Both tokens are transferable within FIFA's controlled ecosystem, but the transfer rules are enforced on-chain, preventing the uncapped resale prices that characterize traditional scalping markets.

Scale and performance

As of mid-June 2026, the FIFA Blockchain had processed more than 60,000 transactions related to ticket rights, with combined trading volume for digital ticket assets surpassing $25 million. Peak activity around major matches pushed Avalanche network traffic up to 24 times its baseline level, a stress test the chain handled without significant congestion.

More than 100,000 digital ticket rights have been issued so far, covering a subset of total World Cup capacity. FIFA has not disclosed the exact proportion of seats managed through the blockchain system.

Invisible blockchain

One of the more significant design choices is that most fans interacting with the system have no idea they are using a blockchain. The purchase flow looks and feels like a standard e-commerce checkout. Wallet creation is abstracted, and asset ownership is surfaced through FIFA Collect's familiar interface. Blockchain handles verification and ownership behind the scenes.

This approach is consistent with how several major consumer platforms have integrated Web3 functionality without requiring users to understand or manage private keys — a pattern also seen in digital collectible programs run by the NBA, Formula 1, and others.

AVAX price reaction

The surge in World Cup-related activity has driven renewed attention to the Avalanche ecosystem, though AVAX's price has not tracked the on-chain activity linearly. The token fell approximately 9.5% in late June amid broader crypto market weakness, breaking the $6.24 support level, even as network usage data pointed to growing real-world adoption. Analysts note that elevated on-chain activity without commensurate token demand reflects a common pattern in enterprise blockchain deployments, where transaction fees are subsidized or minimal.
