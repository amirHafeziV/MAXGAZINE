About

Bitcoin Cash (BCH) is a peer-to-peer digital currency that emerged from a split of the original Bitcoin blockchain, designed with one core goal: to function as practical, everyday electronic cash with low fees and fast confirmation times. Like Bitcoin, it uses a proof-of-work consensus mechanism, where miners compete to validate transactions and secure the network by solving cryptographic puzzles, and it shares Bitcoin's fixed total supply cap of 21 million coins along with a similar halving schedule that reduces mining rewards over time.

The key technical difference from Bitcoin is block size. Bitcoin Cash increased its block size limit substantially compared to Bitcoin's original 1MB blocks, allowing each block to hold many more transactions. The reasoning behind this design choice was that larger blocks would keep transaction fees low and confirmation times fast even as adoption grows, supporting use cases like point-of-sale payments, micropayments and remittances rather than primarily serving as a store of value.

Bitcoin Cash supports smart contract functionality through upgrades like CashScript and has its own ecosystem of wallets, payment processors and merchant tools aimed at making BCH usable for real-world commerce. Its supply, security model and overall architecture remain closely related to Bitcoin's, reflecting its origins as a direct continuation of Bitcoin's codebase with a different philosophy about how the network should scale.

History

Bitcoin Cash was created on August 1, 2017, through a hard fork of the Bitcoin blockchain, the culmination of a years-long debate within the Bitcoin community known as the "block size wars." As Bitcoin's popularity grew, transaction fees rose and confirmation times slowed during periods of high demand, sparking disagreement over how the network should scale. One camp favored increasing the block size limit directly to allow more transactions per block, while another favored off-chain solutions like the Lightning Network while keeping blocks small.

When consensus could not be reached, a group of developers, miners and businesses who favored larger blocks split off to create Bitcoin Cash, which increased the block size limit to 8MB at launch (later raised further) while preserving the existing transaction history up to the point of the fork. Anyone holding Bitcoin at the time of the split received an equal amount of Bitcoin Cash, giving the new chain an instant, distributed user base.

Bitcoin Cash itself later experienced an internal split in November 2018, when disagreements over further protocol changes led to a fork that created Bitcoin SV (Satoshi's Vision) as a separate chain, while Bitcoin Cash continued under its original name and ticker. Since then, Bitcoin Cash has continued to develop independently, implementing regular protocol upgrades aimed at improving transaction throughput, smart contract capability and overall usability as a payments network.

Recent Developments

Bitcoin Cash continues to position itself within the crypto landscape as a low-fee, fast-settlement alternative for payments, appealing to merchants and users who prioritize transaction cost over Bitcoin's role as a store of value. Its community remains active in promoting BCH as a practical means of exchange, supporting integrations with point-of-sale systems, e-commerce plugins and remittance services, particularly in regions where low-cost cross-border transfers matter most.

As the broader conversation around digital payments and stablecoins evolves, Bitcoin Cash's ongoing protocol development focuses on maintaining low fees, improving smart contract capabilities, and ensuring the network remains usable for everyday transactions even as overall blockchain activity fluctuates. Its direct lineage from Bitcoin's original codebase also keeps it relevant in discussions about Bitcoin's scaling history and the different paths blockchain networks can take when communities disagree on technical direction.
