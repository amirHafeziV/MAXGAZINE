Google DeepMind Lost the Transformer Co-Inventor and a Nobel Laureate in Two Consecutive Days

Noam Shazeer, the engineer whose 2017 paper gave the world the architecture running nearly every large AI model today, joined OpenAI on June 18. The following day, Nobel chemistry laureate and AlphaFold creator John Jumper announced he is heading to Anthropic. It is the most concentrated top-tier talent loss in AI industry history.

On June 18, 2026, Noam Shazeer posted to X that he was leaving Google DeepMind to join OpenAI as Lead for Architecture Research. The announcement landed with particular weight: Shazeer is the co-author of the 2017 paper "Attention Is All You Need," which introduced the Transformer architecture — the design underlying virtually every large language model deployed at scale today, including the systems powering OpenAI's own products.

One day later, John Jumper, the computational chemist who led the development of AlphaFold at DeepMind and shared the 2024 Nobel Prize in Chemistry for that work, announced his departure to join Anthropic. Jumper spent nearly nine years at Google DeepMind and his model has mapped more than 200 million protein structures, fundamentally reshaping how drug discovery and biological research are conducted.

The two announcements within 48 hours represent what several prominent AI researchers have described as an unprecedented concentration of high-level talent movement. Taken together, the departures mark the loss of both the architect of modern deep learning infrastructure and the scientist behind what many consider the most important applied AI achievement of the last decade.

Context: A Longer Exodus From Google DeepMind

Shazeer's and Jumper's exits did not happen in isolation. Since early 2026, Google DeepMind has experienced an unusually high rate of senior researcher departures. Shazeer himself had already left Google once before — in 2021, along with fellow researcher Daniel De Freitas — to co-found Character.AI. Google brought him back in August 2024 as part of a $2.7 billion arrangement that gave Google an exclusive licensing deal with Character.AI. Shazeer's decision to leave again, less than two years after that arrangement, signals that financial retention packages alone cannot hold talent indefinitely when rival labs are offering leadership roles in shaping next-generation models.

The departures raise pointed questions about Google's long-term ability to compete at the frontier of AI development. Google DeepMind remains an enormous and technically sophisticated organization, home to several of the most capable foundation models in production — including the Gemini series — and benefits from computational scale that few can match. But the loss of individuals of Shazeer's and Jumper's standing is qualitatively different from typical researcher churn.

What It Means for OpenAI and Anthropic

For OpenAI, Shazeer's arrival reinforces an already-dominant position in language model research. His architectural expertise is directly applicable to the next generation of model design, and his presence gives OpenAI a researcher whose foundational contributions to the field are impossible to replicate by credential-matching.

For Anthropic, Jumper's joining is strategically significant in a different way. Anthropic has been methodically building out an AI-for-science capability throughout 2026: the company opened wet labs, published research on biological AI workflows, and formed partnerships with the Allen Institute and the Howard Hughes Medical Institute. Jumper brings what no benchmark performance metric can supply — a Nobel Prize in Chemistry, deep scientific legitimacy, and a track record of producing tools that are now standard instruments in academic and pharmaceutical research.
