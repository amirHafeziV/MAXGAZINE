BYD Sends Its 150,000-Order Flagship SUV to Europe to Pressure the Premium Incumbents

On June 18, BYD confirmed it will bring the Great Tang — a full-size, seven-seat electric SUV that has already booked 150,000 orders in China — to Europe by late 2026, taking direct aim at Audi, Volvo and Mercedes-Benz on their home turf.

BYD will take its flagship Great Tang electric SUV to Europe, the company's executive vice president Stella Li said in comments reported on June 18, 2026, turning a vehicle that has piled up roughly 150,000 pre-orders in China into the spearhead of the Chinese automaker's push into Europe's premium segment. Deliveries in Europe and the Asia-Pacific region are expected by the end of 2026 or in early 2027.

The Great Tang is a full-size, seven-seat SUV — the largest and most expensive model BYD has built — and it is positioned squarely against established European luxury marques rather than the mass market. Where earlier BYD launches in Europe competed on price at the bottom of the range, the Great Tang is meant to prove the company can challenge brands like Audi, Volvo and Mercedes-Benz on technology and refinement, not just sticker price.

A Technology Showcase

BYD has built the Great Tang around an 800V-class to 1,000V high-voltage architecture and is advertising headline charging speeds that read like a direct rebuttal to range-anxiety concerns: the company says its flash-charging system can take the battery from 10% to 70% in roughly five minutes under ideal conditions, with quoted range figures stretching toward 950 kilometers on China's optimistic CLTC test cycle. Real-world European range, measured on the stricter WLTP standard, will be lower, but the specifications still place the vehicle at the leading edge of mainstream EV engineering.

The SUV also serves as a rolling demonstration of BYD's in-house driver-assistance software. The company has been aggressive about deploying its "God's Eye" advanced driving system across its lineup, and in China has gone as far as pledging to assume financial responsibility for at-fault incidents that occur while the system is engaged — a commitment Western rivals have avoided making.

The European Calculus

Bringing the Great Tang to Europe is as much a manufacturing and policy story as a product one. The European Union imposes a combined import duty of around 27% on battery-electric vehicles shipped from China, which means BYD will have to price the SUV higher in Europe than at home or absorb part of the tariff to stay competitive. To blunt that disadvantage, BYD is ramping up its factory in Hungary, evaluating a second European production site, and expanding regional research and development around autonomous-driving and data capabilities so that more of the value chain sits inside the bloc.

The move lands at a moment of intense global jockeying in electric vehicles. BYD overtook Tesla as the world's top EV seller at the end of 2025, only for Tesla to reclaim the crown in the first quarter of 2026 with 358,023 deliveries. By aiming a credible premium product at Europe's most profitable car-buyers, BYD is signaling that it intends to compete not only on volume but on the high-margin territory that European automakers have long treated as their own.

Whether European buyers embrace a Chinese flagship at premium prices — and whether Brussels tightens trade measures further in response — will shape how far the Great Tang can travel. But the strategic message is unambiguous: BYD no longer wants to be seen as the budget option.
