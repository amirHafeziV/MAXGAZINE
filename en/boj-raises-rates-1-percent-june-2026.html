Bank of Japan Raises Rates to 1% — Highest Since 1995 — as Iran Energy Shock Drives Inflation

The BOJ voted 7-1 on June 16 to raise its benchmark rate to 1.0%, a level not seen in three decades, driven by surging energy costs and a sharp upward revision to the fiscal 2026 core CPI forecast.

The Bank of Japan raised its benchmark short-term interest rate by 25 basis points to 1.0% at its June 16, 2026 policy meeting, the highest level since September 1995. The vote was 7-1, with board member Toichiro Asada the lone dissenter, who cited elevated downside risks to production and employment.

Why Now

The primary driver was an accelerating inflation picture tied to the Iran conflict and its impact on global energy markets. The BOJ simultaneously revised its core Consumer Price Index forecast for fiscal year 2026 upward to a range of 2.5%–3.0%, a sharp climb from the prior estimate of 1.9%. Policymakers argued that allowing inflation expectations to become entrenched carried more risk than tightening prematurely.

The decision also came as expectations mounted that the Strait of Hormuz would reopen — a development that, paradoxically, gave the BOJ more confidence to act by reducing the prospect of a more severe future supply shock.

Market Context

The hike was broadly anticipated: roughly 94% of economists surveyed ahead of the meeting expected a 25bp move. That near-unanimous consensus meant currency and bond markets absorbed the announcement without dramatic volatility. The yen strengthened modestly in the hours following the decision, and Japanese government bond yields edged higher.

For carry trade positions — where investors borrow in low-rate yen and buy higher-yielding assets elsewhere — the cumulative tightening cycle now represents a meaningful cost increase. Since the BOJ began its normalisation path in 2024, the policy rate has moved from -0.1% to 1.0%, a shift that has slowly but steadily narrowed the gap with US and European rates.

What Comes Next

The BOJ's statement retained language suggesting future rate adjustments would remain data-dependent and gradual. Most analysts expect the central bank to pause at 1.0% through at least the end of 2026, barring a further acceleration in energy prices or wages. The next scheduled policy meeting is in late July.

Sources: Bank of Japan statement, CNBC
