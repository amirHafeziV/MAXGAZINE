Ethereum's Usage Hits Records as Fees Collapse — Why the ETH Price Hasn't Followed

First-quarter network data published on June 17 shows monthly active users up nearly 54% to 13.2 million and a record 200 million transactions — even as base-layer fees fell by half, exposing a widening gap between Ethereum's adoption and its token price.

Published 0x Reaserch Team · 2026-06-17
AD SPACEIn-article bannerBook this spot →

Ethereum is being used more than at any point in its history, yet that surge in activity is no longer translating into a higher token price. Quarterly network metrics published on June 17, 2026 show the network setting fresh adoption records across almost every operational measure — while the economics that once linked usage to ETH's value have quietly broken down.

The records, in numbers

Monthly active users climbed to an all-time high of 13.2 million in the first quarter, up 53.5% from the previous quarter and roughly 86% higher than a year earlier. Total transactions reached a record 200.4 million, and average throughput rose to about 25.8 transactions per second. By the standard yardsticks of network health — users, transactions, and capacity — Ethereum has never been busier.

Why fees fell as usage rose

The counterintuitive part is what happened to fees. Base-layer transaction fees fell nearly 50% to about $39.9 million for the quarter, even as activity hit records. The driver is technical: the Fusaka upgrade, which went live in December 2025, expanded the network's data capacity through PeerDAS and a second "Blob Parameters Only" adjustment that raised the number of data blobs each block can carry. More capacity means rollups and applications pay far less to post their data on-chain, pushing the cost of using Ethereum sharply lower.

The investment paradox

For years, the bull case for ETH rested on a simple loop: more usage means more fees burned, which means a tighter supply and a higher price. Fusaka deliberately weakened that loop. Cheaper data is excellent for users and for the layer-2 networks built on top of Ethereum, but it removes much of the fee pressure that previously supported the token. The result is the gap on display this quarter — record adoption alongside an ETH price that opened around $1,700 in late June, far below its prior highs.

What it signals for the rest of 2026

The data reframes how Ethereum should be judged. As a settlement layer and a platform for tokenized assets and decentralized finance, the network is thriving and increasingly cheap to use. As an asset, ETH now depends less on raw transaction fees and more on other sources of value — staking demand, its role as collateral across DeFi, and growth in the layer-2 ecosystem it underpins. The next upgrade on the roadmap, Glamsterdam, is expected to push capacity higher still, which would deepen rather than close the divide between Ethereum the network and ETH the token.
