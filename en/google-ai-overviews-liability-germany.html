A German Court Says Google Owns What Its AI Says — and the Reasoning Could Reach Every Answer Engine

In a June ruling, the Regional Court of Munich held that Google's AI Overviews are Google's own statements — not third-party search results — making the company directly liable for false claims they generate. The decision is preliminary, but its logic points straight at the entire AI-answer industry.

A German court has drawn one of the sharpest legal lines yet around generative AI: if a company's AI writes it, the company is responsible for it. The Regional Court of Munich issued a preliminary injunction barring Google from spreading false statements about two Munich-based publishers through its AI-generated search summaries, in a case docketed as 26 O 869/26. According to the court, Google's "AI Overviews" had falsely linked the publishers to scams, subscription traps and dubious business practices — connections that did not appear in any of the sources the AI cited.

What makes the ruling significant is not the specific falsehood but the court's classification of who is speaking. Judges treated the AI Overview as Google's own content rather than a neutral list of links, and therefore held the company liable as a direct infringer. The court's reasoning was blunt: Google built the AI, Google offers it to users, and Google alone controls the algorithms behind it — so Google owns what it produces.

Why This Breaks From Search-Engine Law

For two decades, courts in many jurisdictions have shielded search engines from liability for what their indexes surface, on the theory that the operator is merely pointing to third-party material it did not author. The Munich court found that this protection does not extend to AI-generated answers, because an Overview is not a pointer to someone else's words — it is a new statement composed by Google's system. That distinction collapses the legal shield that has underpinned the search business model, at least for the AI layer now sitting on top of it.

The implication runs well beyond Google. If a generated answer counts as the provider's own speech, the same exposure would attach to any company running an answer engine — from ChatGPT to Perplexity — whenever its model states something false about a real person or business. The court itself noted that its reasoning could carry international reach.

The Important Caveats

This is a preliminary injunction from a regional court, not a final judgment or binding national precedent. Germany operates under a civil-law system, so the decision does not bind other courts the way precedent does in common-law countries, and Google can appeal. The ruling addresses one dispute between two publishers and one company, and a higher court could narrow or overturn it.

Even so, the direction of travel matters. Defamation and false-statement claims against AI outputs have been multiplying as chatbots and AI summaries move to the center of how people find information, and regulators in the EU have been the most willing to treat AI providers as responsible for their systems' outputs. A ruling that an AI answer is the provider's own speech — if it survives appeal — would reshape the risk calculus for every company that puts a generative summary between a user and the open web. For now, it is a warning shot: the era in which platforms could disclaim what their software says may be closing.
