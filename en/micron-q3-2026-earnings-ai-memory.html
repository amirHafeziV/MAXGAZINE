Micron Posts $41 Billion Quarter as AI Memory Demand Makes Its Financials Unrecognizable

Micron Technology reported Q3 fiscal 2026 revenue of $41.46 billion — a figure that is four times higher than a year ago — with gross margins at 85% and earnings per share of $24.67. Customers have committed $22 billion in fixed-price contracts to secure future supply, and Q4 guidance points to $50 billion. The AI memory supercycle is no longer a prediction.

Micron Technology reported fiscal third-quarter 2026 results after market close on June 24 that exceeded every analyst forecast by a dramatic margin. Revenue came in at $41.46 billion — against analyst consensus expectations of approximately $35.9 billion — representing roughly fourfold growth year over year from what had been, just twelve months ago, a deeply depressed memory chip market.

Gross margins expanded to 85%, lifting net income to $28.24 billion compared with $1.88 billion in the same quarter of 2025. Diluted earnings per share reached $24.67, compared with $1.68 a year earlier. Micron shares jumped approximately 14.6% in after-hours trading on the results.

The AI Memory Supercycle in Hard Numbers

The figures are a direct measure of what AI infrastructure spending has done to the memory chip market. Data center revenue exceeded $25 billion in the quarter — equivalent to an annualized run rate above $100 billion — driven primarily by High Bandwidth Memory (HBM) modules that are integral components of the AI accelerators made by Nvidia, AMD, and Google's custom TPUs.

Micron also disclosed that customers have committed approximately $22 billion in fixed-price supply contracts to secure memory allocations, a figure that indicates large hyperscalers are not treating AI memory access as a spot market but as critical infrastructure that must be locked in at the source.

Data center SSD revenue exceeded $5 billion in the quarter and more than doubled sequentially, reflecting the parallel expansion in AI storage infrastructure alongside compute.

Guidance That Rewrites the Scale of Expectations

Micron guided Q4 fiscal 2026 revenue to approximately $50 billion, against analyst estimates of roughly $44 billion and a year-ago figure of just over $11 billion. If achieved, that would make Micron's fiscal year 2026 the most profitable in the company's four-decade history by a significant margin, and would place annual memory chip revenue in territory that seemed implausible to most semiconductor analysts as recently as 2024.

The company also secured 16 multi-year strategic supply agreements with data center operators and automotive companies during the quarter, representing an estimated $100 billion in lifetime value — a figure that provides unusually long forward visibility for what has historically been a highly cyclical commodity business.

What This Means for the Broader AI Infrastructure Race

Micron's results are a benchmark for understanding how deep and durable the current AI capital expenditure cycle actually is. The company competes against Samsung Electronics and SK hynix, both of which have recently announced their own AI-memory partnership agreements. Nvidia itself disclosed a multiyear co-development agreement with SK hynix during the same week, aimed at aligning next-generation memory specifications directly with Nvidia's roadmap.

For the technology sector broadly, Micron's quarter is a reminder that the economic value being created by AI is not concentrated only in model companies and applications — it is flowing heavily into foundational silicon, and the companies that make the hardware enabling AI inference and training are seeing the financial effects of that at a scale that has no precedent in the industry's history.
