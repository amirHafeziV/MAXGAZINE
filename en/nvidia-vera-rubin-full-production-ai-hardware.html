Nvidia's Vera Rubin Enters Full Production as the Next AI Hardware Cycle Begins

At Computex 2026 in Taipei, Nvidia said its next-generation Vera Rubin platform has reached full production, with shipments due in the fall — a launch CEO Jensen Huang called one of the largest in Taiwan's history.

Nvidia has moved its next major artificial-intelligence platform from announcement to assembly line. Speaking at Computex 2026 in Taipei, held June 1–5, the company said its Vera Rubin platform has entered full production, with shipments to partners beginning in the fall. Chief Executive Jensen Huang framed it as a milestone for the whole industry, calling Vera Rubin "the largest product launch, probably in the history of Taiwan."

What Vera Rubin actually is

Rubin is not a single chip but a tightly integrated platform of six. First unveiled at CES in January 2026, it pairs the new Vera CPU with Rubin GPUs and a stack of supporting silicon — the NVLink 6 switch, ConnectX-9 SuperNIC, BlueField-4 DPU and Spectrum-6 Ethernet switch — designed to work as one rack-scale system. A single Vera Rubin processor combines one Vera CPU with two Rubin GPUs, and Huang said each full system is built from nearly two million parts and draws on roughly 150 ecosystem partners in Taiwan alone.

A supercycle, not a single release

The significance is less about any one benchmark than about scale and timing. Nvidia says Rubin-based products will be available from partners in the second half of 2026, with major cloud providers — including AWS, Google Cloud, Microsoft and Oracle's OCI, alongside specialist operators such as CoreWeave, Lambda, Nebius and Nscale — among the first to deploy Vera Rubin instances. That breadth of early adoption signals how quickly the platform is expected to become the backbone of new AI data centers.

Taiwan moves up the value chain

Huang's framing of the launch as historic for Taiwan reflects a broader shift. The island is no longer only manufacturing the world's AI hardware; through deep design and ecosystem partnerships, it is increasingly helping define it. The roughly 150 partners involved in building each Rubin system underline how central the local supply chain has become to the global AI build-out.

Why it matters beyond the data center

For the wider market, Rubin reaching full production sets the pace for the next phase of AI investment. The companies racing to train and run ever-larger models depend on exactly this kind of hardware, and the timing of fall shipments will shape capacity — and costs — heading into 2027. After a year dominated by news about AI software and model releases, the hardware underneath that boom is back at the center of the story.
