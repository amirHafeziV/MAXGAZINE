Binance to Suspend EU Services July 1 After MiCA License Failure

The world's largest crypto exchange will halt new orders, deposits, and staking for EU residents after withdrawing its MiCA application in Greece. Binance says it plans to reapply in France and return once licensed.

Published MAXGAZINE Desk · 2026-06-26
AD SPACEIn-article bannerBook this spot →

Binance, the world's largest cryptocurrency exchange by trading volume, has notified users across the European Union that it will suspend the majority of its services on July 1, 2026, after failing to obtain a Markets in Crypto-Assets (MiCA) license before the bloc's regulatory deadline.

The company submitted a MiCA application in January 2026 through a Greek holding entity called Binary Greece, targeting the Hellenic Capital Market Commission (HCMC) as its regulatory gateway into the EU. The HCMC never issued a formal approval. On June 25, Binance withdrew the application, citing "the timeline of the process" and concerns raised by regulators around anti-money laundering controls and executive "fit and proper" assessments.

From July 1, EU residents will be unable to open new accounts, place new orders, make deposits, or access staking products on Binance. Existing users will, however, retain full access to their funds and be able to withdraw assets freely — a point Binance stressed in its communications to customers.

France as the next bid

Binance has publicly committed to pursuing a MiCA license in France, where it previously held a digital-asset service provider registration before EU-wide rules took precedence. The company said it remains "confident" it will secure an EU-wide authorization in the coming months, though any approval is now certain to arrive after the July 1 suspension takes effect.

The MiCA framework, which came into full force in January 2026 after a phased rollout that began in June 2024, requires crypto asset service providers operating across the EU to obtain a single license in one member state. That license then grants passporting rights across the entire bloc.

Market implications

The EU represents a substantial portion of Binance's retail user base. Analysts estimate that blocking EU customer access — even temporarily — could redirect billions of dollars in monthly trading volume toward licensed competitors such as Coinbase, Bitvavo, and Kraken, all of which hold MiCA authorizations.

Binance's difficulties also underscore the stricter enforcement posture regulators have adopted since the collapse of FTX in late 2022. The HCMC's concerns about anti-money laundering procedures mirror objections raised by regulators in other jurisdictions, including the US Department of Justice, which reached a $4.3 billion settlement with Binance in November 2023.
