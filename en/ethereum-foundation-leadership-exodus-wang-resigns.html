Ethereum Foundation Loses Another Top Leader as Co-Director Hsiao-Wei Wang Resigns

Hsiao-Wei Wang, who helped steer Ethereum's switch to proof-of-stake, stepped down as co-executive director effective June 18 — the latest in a string of senior departures that has reignited debate over the Foundation's governance and direction.

Hsiao-Wei Wang has resigned as co-executive director of the Ethereum Foundation, the nonprofit that supports development of the world's second-largest blockchain, with her departure effective June 18, 2026. Wang, who joined Ethereum's research team in 2017 and helped shape the network's landmark transition to proof-of-stake, said a recent sabbatical had given her time to reflect on her future priorities.

Her exit follows the earlier resignation of fellow co-executive director Tomasz Stańczak and leaves Bastian Aue as the sole acting director. By the count of several crypto outlets, Wang is among roughly eight senior figures to have left the Foundation over the past five months, a pace of turnover that has drawn sustained scrutiny of how the organization is run.

A Governance Question, Not a Price Story

The departures matter less for what they do to Ether's price on any given day than for what they signal about stewardship of a network that secures hundreds of billions of dollars in value and underpins much of decentralized finance. The Ethereum Foundation does not control the protocol — Ethereum is maintained by a broad, independent community of developers — but it remains the most visible institution funding core research, coordinating upgrades, and representing the ecosystem in policy discussions.

A thinning leadership bench raises practical questions about continuity at a moment when Ethereum is juggling several priorities at once: scaling through layer-2 networks, defending its position against faster rival chains, and adapting to a regulatory environment that is finally taking clearer shape in the United States and Europe.

Restructuring in the Background

The leadership changes have unfolded alongside a broader reorganization at the Foundation, which has moved over the past year to sharpen its mandate, separate research from day-to-day operations, and respond to long-running community criticism that it was opaque and slow to act. Supporters frame the turnover as the natural cost of that restructuring — old leaders making way as the institution redefines its role. Critics see an organization struggling to retain experienced talent at a pivotal time.

Wang, for her part, framed her decision in personal terms rather than as a rebuke, and contributors of her seniority typically remain active in the ecosystem in other capacities even after leaving formal roles. Whether the recent exits ultimately read as a healthy reset or a worrying drain will depend on how quickly the Foundation fills the gaps — and on whether Ethereum's next round of upgrades stays on schedule.
