About Kaspa
Trade Now →

Kaspa (KAS) is a proof-of-work cryptocurrency built on a BlockDAG (Block Directed Acyclic Graph) architecture, designed to solve one of the fundamental tensions in blockchain design: the trade-off between speed, security, and decentralization. Traditional proof-of-work blockchains like Bitcoin produce one block at a time, and blocks that arrive "out of order" — called orphan blocks — are simply discarded. This limits throughput and forces a choice between frequent but possibly insecure blocks, or rare but secure ones.

Kaspa's architecture rejects this compromise. Instead of a linear chain where only one block can extend the ledger at a time, Kaspa uses a BlockDAG structure where multiple blocks can be created and confirmed in parallel. The GHOSTDAG consensus protocol then organizes all of these parallel blocks into a consistent, universally agreed-upon order, without discarding valid work.

The GHOSTDAG Protocol

GHOSTDAG (Greedy Heaviest-Observed Sub-Tree Directed Acyclic Graph) is the consensus mechanism at the heart of Kaspa. It is a practical implementation of the theoretical PHANTOM protocol, designed to efficiently classify honest, well-connected blocks and produce a total ordering of all blocks in the DAG. Unlike Nakamoto consensus — which discards competing blocks and counts only the longest chain — GHOSTDAG incorporates all legitimate blocks into the ordering, rewarding honest miners for their work even when their blocks arrive at the same time as another miner's.

This approach allows Kaspa to run at dramatically higher block rates than Bitcoin while maintaining proof-of-work security. At launch, Kaspa targeted one block per second (1 BPS). After the Crescendo Hard Fork activated in May 2025, this increased to 10 BPS, with plans to push further toward 32 BPS and eventually 100 BPS.

Fair Launch and Tokenomics

Kaspa launched in November 2021 with a completely fair launch: no premine, no ICO, no venture capital allocation, and no founder token reserve. This design echoes Bitcoin's original ethos — making the network's early participants the same as any later participant, with no insider advantage. The emission schedule follows a smooth, decreasing curve inspired by Bitcoin's halving model, but designed to reduce more gradually over time.

The intellectual origins of Kaspa stretch back years before its launch. The GHOSTDAG protocol is the result of a long line of academic research on DAG-based consensus mechanisms, with roots in work conducted at the Hebrew University of Jerusalem and later connected to research at Harvard University. The theoretical groundwork includes the GHOST protocol (referenced in the original Ethereum white paper), the SPECTRE protocol, and the PHANTOM protocol — each a step toward solving the problem of parallel block production in a secure consensus environment.

Academic Foundations

Yonatan Sompolinsky is one of the key researchers credited with foundational work on DAG-based blockchain consensus. His co-authorship on the PHANTOM and SPECTRE papers laid the groundwork for what would eventually become the GHOSTDAG protocol running Kaspa. The transition from academic theory to working blockchain required not just theoretical correctness but efficient, production-ready implementations capable of handling real network conditions.

Mainnet Launch (November 2021)

Kaspa launched its mainnet in November 2021 with a philosophy designed to echo Bitcoin's original fairness principles: no pre-allocation of tokens, no institutional investors, and no foundation holding a token reserve. Mining began at launch, open to anyone with the appropriate hardware. This fair launch was a deliberate choice to build credibility in a space where many new projects were criticized for insider token allocations.

The network attracted an early community of proof-of-work enthusiasts, researchers and developers who were drawn to the technical ambition of achieving high block rates without sacrificing decentralization. The project maintained an open-source development model, with the core protocol and client software published and available for community review and contribution.

Growing Recognition (2022–2023)

Through 2022 and into 2023, Kaspa gained significant recognition within the cryptocurrency community for its technical design. As many proof-of-stake networks faced debates around centralization and validator dynamics, Kaspa's commitment to proof-of-work — with its well-understood security model — attracted users who valued the properties of a mining-based consensus.

The network also attracted mining hardware manufacturers who began developing and selling ASIC miners purpose-built for the KAS mining algorithm (kHeavyHash). This transition from GPU mining to ASIC mining was a milestone: it signaled that KAS had achieved enough economic significance to justify specialized hardware investment, while also raising community discussions about mining decentralization.

Crescendo Hard Fork (May 2025)

The Crescendo Hard Fork, activated in May 2025, was one of the most significant technical milestones in Kaspa's history. It increased the block rate from 1 block per second to 10 blocks per second, while proportionally reducing the reward per block to maintain the same per-second emission rate. This upgrade demonstrated the network's ability to execute major protocol changes through community consensus and validated the scalability roadmap that the project had outlined since its early days.

At 10 BPS, transactions are included in a block within roughly 100 milliseconds, with first confirmation in about one second and full probabilistic security (comparable to Bitcoin's six-confirmation standard) reached in approximately 10 seconds.

Kaspa's present-day story is one of the more technically ambitious in the proof-of-work blockchain space. Following the successful activation of the Crescendo Hard Fork in May 2025, the network operates at 10 blocks per second — a rate that was considered theoretical for proof-of-work just a few years prior. The development roadmap points toward further block rate increases, with targets of 32 BPS and eventually 100 BPS on the horizon.

Roadmap: Towards Higher Throughput

The path to higher block rates involves ongoing improvements to the GHOSTDAG implementation, node software optimizations and network propagation efficiency. Each increase in block rate must be carefully balanced against the underlying security model — ensuring that the faster block production does not compromise the properties that make proof-of-work resistant to attack. The Kaspa development community has maintained a technically rigorous approach to these upgrades, prioritizing correctness over speed-to-deployment.

ASIC Mining and Decentralization Dynamics

The arrival of ASIC miners for Kaspa created a dynamic familiar from Bitcoin's history: specialized hardware improves mining efficiency and network security, but also concentrates mining power in the hands of those who can afford or manufacture the hardware. The Kaspa community continues to monitor and discuss mining decentralization as ASIC adoption grows, balancing the network security benefits of higher hashrate against the philosophical commitment to accessible mining.

Market Position and Community

Kaspa has built a passionate community of proof-of-work advocates who value its technical differentiation from both Bitcoin (which does not target high throughput) and proof-of-stake networks (which achieve speed through different security assumptions). Its fair launch, lack of venture backing, and open-source development ethos resonate with users who are skeptical of heavily pre-allocated or VC-backed projects.

The KAS token's market position reflects this community: it trades as a relatively high-liquidity proof-of-work asset and is listed on major exchanges. Kaspa represents a bet that proof-of-work blockchains can remain technically competitive in the scaling debate — that the properties of mining-based security do not have to be traded away for speed.
