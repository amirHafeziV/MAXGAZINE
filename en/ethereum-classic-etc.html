About

Ethereum Classic (ETC) is the original Ethereum blockchain, continuing along the unmodified chain that preceded the 2016 hard fork that created today's Ethereum (ETH). It supports smart contracts and decentralised applications in the same way Ethereum does, using the Ethereum Virtual Machine to execute code and allowing developers to build self-executing contracts and decentralised protocols.

The fundamental distinction between Ethereum Classic and Ethereum lies in philosophy rather than technology. ETC adheres to the principle of blockchain immutability — the idea that a blockchain's transaction history, once confirmed, should never be altered regardless of circumstances. This made ETC the continuation of the original chain after the Ethereum community chose to reverse a major theft through a hard fork, while ETC proponents refused the change and maintained the original ledger as authoritative.

ETC uses a proof-of-work consensus mechanism, unlike Ethereum which switched to proof-of-stake in 2022. Miners use computing power to validate transactions and earn block rewards denominated in ETC. The supply of ETC is capped through a programmatic reduction schedule that reduces block rewards over time — similar in concept to Bitcoin's halving — placing a hard cap on the total coins that will ever exist. This fixed-supply model, combined with its proof-of-work roots, has attracted comparison to Bitcoin as a form of digital commodity.

History

Ethereum Classic's origins trace directly to a crisis that struck the early Ethereum ecosystem in June 2016. A project known as The DAO — a decentralised autonomous organisation that had raised more than $150 million in ETH through a crowdfunding campaign — was exploited by an attacker who drained approximately one-third of its funds through a vulnerability in the smart contract code. The Ethereum community faced a stark choice: allow the theft to stand, preserving the blockchain's immutable record, or execute a hard fork to return the funds to investors.

The majority of the Ethereum community, including most of its core developers and co-founder Vitalik Buterin, supported the hard fork. It was executed in July 2016, creating what became the main Ethereum chain (ETH). However, a minority of participants rejected this intervention on ideological grounds, arguing that the principle of "code is law" — the idea that smart contracts, once deployed, should run as written without external correction — must be upheld even in the face of a hack. This group continued mining the original chain, giving rise to Ethereum Classic.

In the years following the split, ETC developed its own independent community, developer base and ecosystem. It navigated several significant 51% attacks between 2019 and 2020, where attackers temporarily controlled the majority of the network's hash rate and successfully double-spent coins — a serious challenge to the security of proof-of-work chains with lower hash rates. The ETC community and infrastructure providers responded with technical improvements including increased confirmation requirements and checkpointing mechanisms.

The network also implemented its own emission reduction schedule — sometimes called "fifthening" — to place a programmatic cap on total supply, and continued to receive protocol upgrades while maintaining compatibility with its proof-of-work foundation even as the broader Ethereum ecosystem moved away from it.

Recent Developments

Ethereum Classic today occupies a distinct niche in the cryptocurrency ecosystem — a proof-of-work smart contract platform that emphasises immutability and decentralisation above all. While it lacks the application ecosystem, developer activity and institutional momentum of Ethereum, it attracts a base of supporters who value the ideological commitment to an unaltered blockchain history.

The switch by Ethereum to proof-of-stake in 2022 — known as The Merge — had a notable effect on ETC. Miners who had previously secured the Ethereum network moved significant hash rate to Ethereum Classic, substantially increasing the network's hash rate and, by extension, its resistance to 51% attacks. This influx of mining power strengthened the security argument for ETC as the most prominent proof-of-work smart contract platform.

The ETC Cooperative and other organisations continue to support development activity on the network, working on protocol improvements and tooling. Ethereum Classic maintains support for the full EVM instruction set, meaning contracts and tools written for Ethereum can generally be deployed on ETC with minimal modification. This compatibility positions it as a stable, immutable alternative layer for projects that prioritise permanence and censorship-resistance. For long-term holders, ETC's fixed supply, proof-of-work foundation and its principled history give it a value proposition distinct from any other smart contract platform.
