The Great AI IPO Wave: OpenAI and Anthropic Both File for Public Markets — But the Numbers Are Uncomfortable

Within days of each other in early June, OpenAI and Anthropic both filed confidential S-1 paperwork with the SEC. Together they are targeting valuations above $1.8 trillion — while collectively burning roughly $40 billion per year.

Published MAXGAZINE Desk · 2026-06-09
AD SPACEIn-article bannerBook this spot →

In the span of a week in early June 2026, the two most prominent AI companies in the world both took formal steps toward going public — a convergence that signals both the maturing of the generative AI industry and the enormous appetite of institutional investors for exposure to it.

Anthropic filed its S-1 with the SEC in late May / early June, receiving a market valuation of approximately $965 billion. OpenAI followed on June 8–9, 2026, confirming a confidential submission but stating that IPO timing "may be a while," with internal projections suggesting a window in the September–Q4 2026 timeframe.

The Revenue Story

The growth numbers are remarkable. OpenAI reached $25 billion in annualized revenue by February 2026, up from $20 billion at the end of 2025, growing at roughly $2 billion per month. Goldman Sachs, Morgan Stanley, and JPMorgan are underwriting the offering, targeting a valuation near $1 trillion — which would make it the largest IPO in history if achieved at the top end.

Anthropic, with annualized revenue approaching $19 billion, is valued at approximately $965 billion. The two companies combined would represent a combined implied market value that dwarfs traditional software companies at equivalent revenue stages.

The Problem Nobody Wants to Talk About

Beneath the headline revenue numbers lies a structural concern: both companies are burning cash at extraordinary rates. OpenAI's projected cash burn for 2026 is approximately $27 billion against $25 billion in revenue — meaning the company is losing roughly $1.22 for every dollar it earns. Anthropic's economics are similarly challenged.

The burn rates reflect the enormous cost of training frontier AI models, running inference at scale, and competing for AI research talent. Neither company has demonstrated a credible path to positive free cash flow within the next two years. Investors in their IPOs will essentially be betting that revenue growth continues to accelerate fast enough to eventually outrun costs.

What This Means for the Industry

The parallel IPO filings mark a generational moment for AI: the shift from private, investor-backed research organizations to publicly accountable companies with quarterly earnings calls and institutional shareholders. That change will impose new disciplines — and new pressures — on both firms.

For the broader tech market, the question is whether public investors will price these companies on current cash flows (which are deeply negative) or on projections of dominance in a category worth tens of trillions over the next decade. The answer will set the benchmark valuation for every AI company that follows them to market.
