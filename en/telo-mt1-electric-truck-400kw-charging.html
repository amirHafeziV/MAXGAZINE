A Tiny Electric Truck With a Big Trick: Telo's MT1 Promises Sustained 400 kW Charging

Startup Telo confirmed on June 19 that its city-sized MT1 pickup will sustain 400 kW DC charging — not just peak at it — thanks to a dual-voltage split battery pack. The truck is roughly the length of a Mini but carries a Tacoma-sized bed, starting near $41,500.

An electric pickup most American buyers have never heard of is making one of the boldest charging claims in the industry. On June 19, 2026, California startup Telo Trucks confirmed that its compact MT1 will sustain a 400 kW DC charging rate rather than merely touching that figure for a few seconds before tapering — a distinction that, if it holds up in production, would put a tiny truck ahead of far more expensive EVs.

The split-pack trick

The engineering behind the claim is a dual-voltage battery design. The MT1 uses two 400-volt packs that can run in parallel at 400 volts or switch into series for 800-volt charging. That lets the truck draw full power from the high-output 800-volt chargers now appearing on highways while still performing well on the far more common 400-volt fast chargers that make up most of today's network. Sustained 400 kW means the battery holds a high charging rate across much of the session instead of peaking briefly, which is what actually determines how long a driver waits.

Small outside, useful inside

The MT1's other selling point is its packaging. At about 152 inches long it is shorter than a two-door Mini Cooper, yet Telo says it offers the same interior space and bed length as a Toyota Tacoma — an attempt to solve the contradiction of modern trucks, which have grown enormous even as buyers complain they no longer fit in cities or garages. Power options run from a 300-horsepower rear-drive single motor to a 500-horsepower dual-motor all-wheel-drive version that Telo claims hits 60 mph in about four seconds.

Range, price, and the catch

The standard 77-kWh pack targets about 260 miles of range, with an optional 106-kWh pack rated near 350 miles. Pricing starts around $41,520 for the base model and roughly $45,500 for the longer-range version. The catch is the timeline and the company: Telo is a startup, low-volume production is only expected to begin in late 2026, and first deliveries are targeted at roughly 500 units. As any EV startup's history shows, the gap between a confident specification sheet and vehicles in customers' driveways is where many ambitious projects stumble.

Why it matters

The MT1 is a test of two ideas at once: that there is real demand for a genuinely small electric truck, and that clever battery architecture can deliver charging performance once reserved for premium vehicles. If Telo can ship a sub-$42,000 truck that charges as fast as it claims, it would pressure both legacy automakers, who have struggled to build affordable compact EVs, and the broader assumption that fast charging has to be expensive. The number to watch is not the 400 kW headline but whether the company can turn its specification into deliveries on schedule.
