Global Markets Surge as US and Iran Announce Ceasefire Deal

Stock markets worldwide rallied sharply on June 15 after the US and Iran agreed to a ceasefire framework, sending crude oil to its lowest since mid-April and triggering a broad risk-on wave across equities and crypto.

Published 2026-06-15 · MAXGAZINE Desk

Global equity markets staged a sharp rally on Monday, June 15, after the United States and Iran announced a ceasefire framework that paused months of conflict and signaled the reopening of the Strait of Hormuz to commercial shipping. US President Donald Trump announced the deal on Sunday evening, confirming the immediate removal of the US naval blockade on Iranian ports and authorizing toll-free passage through the Strait.

By the close of trading on June 15, the S&P 500 had advanced 1.83% to 7,567, the Nasdaq Composite rose 2.87% to 26,631, and the Dow Jones Industrial Average gained 1.34% to 51,888. Asian and European markets had already moved sharply higher overnight ahead of the US open.

Oil Falls as Hormuz Reopens

Crude oil absorbed the largest repricing. Brent crude fell to approximately $80 per barrel — its lowest since mid-April — as traders priced in the prospect of restored Iranian oil export capacity and eased logistical bottlenecks through the Strait, which carries roughly 20% of global oil supply when fully operational. Lower energy costs are broadly positive for corporate margins and inflation expectations.

Crypto Markets Join the Rally

Digital assets participated in the broader risk-on move. Bitcoin opened June 15 at approximately $65,710, up around 2% from the prior day, advancing to $66,157 by mid-morning New York time. Ethereum recovered to $1,724, a gain of roughly 2.6%. The ceasefire announcement helped partially reverse a multi-week slide in crypto markets that analysts had attributed partly to geopolitical uncertainty and institutional risk-off behavior.

FOMC Meeting Adds Context

The rally unfolded at the start of the Federal Reserve's June 16–17 FOMC meeting, where markets assigned near-zero probability to a rate change; the federal funds target rate was expected to remain at 3.5–3.75%. Fed Chair Kevin Warsh, who took office in May 2026, chairs his second formal FOMC session. Investors were watching Wednesday's policy statement for any language suggesting a more hawkish tilt, given inflation remaining above 4%.

For global investors, the ceasefire represented a meaningful clearing of a risk overhang that had weighed on sentiment, suppressed appetite for risk assets, and kept energy prices elevated for more than three months.
