Lucid Motors Cuts 18% of Workforce in Second Mass Layoff of 2026, Eliminates COO Role

Lucid Group announced on June 22, 2026 that it is cutting roughly 1,500 employees — about 18% of its total workforce — as new CEO Silvio Napoli's first major strategic decision, while also eliminating the COO position following Marc Winterhoff's departure. The cuts are expected to generate $158 million in annualized savings.

Lucid Group filed an 8-K with the SEC and issued a company statement on June 22, 2026 announcing the elimination of approximately 1,500 full-time employees, contractors, and hourly production workers — representing roughly 18% of its global workforce and its second major workforce reduction in less than five months, following a 12% cut announced in February 2026.

Leadership Changes Behind the Cuts

The restructuring is the first significant act by Silvio Napoli, who formally became Lucid's CEO on June 1, 2026 after serving in a similar role at Schindler Group. Napoli's arrival ended a prolonged period of leadership uncertainty: Marc Winterhoff, who served as interim CEO for more than a year, exited effective immediately alongside the announcement, and the COO role he held has been permanently eliminated.

The restructuring plan was framed in the 8-K as a measure "designed to advance the Company's path toward profitability and positive cash flow generation by streamlining its organizational structure." Lucid's market value was approximately $2 billion at the time of the announcement, with the stock trading near $5.18 — representing a decline of more than 66% during Winterhoff's interim tenure.

Production and Financial Impact

Alongside the workforce reduction, Lucid is eliminating the second production shift at its Casa Grande, Arizona manufacturing plant, the facility where it builds the Lucid Air luxury sedan. The Arizona Department of Commerce received notice that 705 jobs in the state will be eliminated.

Lucid expects to incur one-time severance and restructuring costs of approximately $32 million. Annualized savings from the cuts are projected at $158 million. The company lost more than $1 billion in the first quarter of 2026 alone and suspended its full-year delivery guidance earlier in the year.

Context: Lucid's Widening Struggle

Lucid entered 2026 as one of several premium EV startups — alongside Rivian and Fisker (which entered bankruptcy in early 2025) — that have struggled to translate product quality into sustainable production volumes. The Lucid Air, despite consistently earning praise for its best-in-class range (exceeding 500 miles on a single charge for premium variants) and interior quality, has failed to generate enough demand to reach the production scale needed for profitability.

Delivery targets have been repeatedly revised downward. Lucid suspended full-year 2026 guidance in February, when it also made the first round of layoffs, citing macro headwinds and an EV market in which consumer demand has shifted disproportionately toward more affordable models.

The company's situation contrasts with its Saudi backers' continued commitment: Lucid's primary investor, the Public Investment Fund (PIF) of Saudi Arabia, has provided repeated capital infusions and is funding the construction of a manufacturing plant in King Abdullah Economic City, where the planned Lucid Cosmos midsize SUV will eventually be built. That vehicle, aimed at competing with the Tesla Model Y, is expected to enter production in late 2026.

What the Cuts Signal for the EV Industry

Lucid's second layoff in five months is part of a broader pattern in the EV startup sector. Across the industry, companies that launched with premium positioning are under mounting pressure as the mass-market shift to EVs has been slower than projected and as Tesla and Chinese manufacturers (BYD, Li Auto, Xpeng) have compressed margins at the volume end of the market.

For investors, the elimination of the COO role and the consolidation of leadership under Napoli signals an attempt to simplify decision-making and reduce overhead. Whether the resulting structure can deliver the Cosmos on schedule and ramp production to profitability will likely define Lucid's viability as an independent company through 2027.
