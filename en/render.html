About

Render (RENDER) is the native token of the Render Network, a decentralized platform that connects people and organizations who need GPU computing power with owners of GPUs who have spare capacity to offer. The network is designed primarily for 3D rendering — the computationally intensive process of generating photorealistic images and animations from 3D models — but has expanded its scope to include AI inference, machine learning workloads and other GPU-intensive computation.

Think of it as a marketplace for GPU cycles: an artist or studio that needs to render a complex 3D scene can access distributed GPU nodes through Render Network, often at lower cost and with greater flexibility than renting capacity from a centralized cloud provider. GPU node operators, in turn, earn RENDER tokens for the work their hardware contributes to the network.

Origins: OTOY and Blockchain Rendering

The Render Network was conceived by Jules Urbach, the founder and CEO of OTOY Inc., a company with a long history in cloud rendering, holographic technology and media production tools. OTOY's OctaneRender is one of the leading GPU rendering engines used in film, television and visual effects production, giving the Render Network deep roots in professional creative industry workflows.

Urbach first conceived of a distributed GPU rendering network as early as 2009, and the concept was brought to blockchain through OTOY's work over the following years. The network held its initial token sale in late 2017, with early access beginning in 2018 and the mainnet publicly launching on April 27, 2020.

RENDER Token and the Burn-and-Mint Model

In late 2023, the Render Network community approved a shift from the original fixed-price payment model to a Burn-and-Mint Equilibrium (BME) model. Under this system, clients who pay for rendering work spend RENDER tokens, which are burned. Node operators who complete that work receive newly minted RENDER as compensation. This model ties token supply dynamics directly to network usage: more rendering jobs mean more burning and more minting, creating a supply mechanism anchored in real economic activity rather than pure speculation.

Scale and Infrastructure

As of 2026, the Render Network has processed tens of millions of frames for clients ranging from independent artists to major studios. The network has supported thousands of active GPU nodes worldwide, and its integration with professional tools and pipelines — including compatibility with OctaneRender — means that new users can often access the network without fundamentally changing their existing creative workflows.

History

The roots of the Render Network trace back to OTOY Inc., the media technology company founded by Jules Urbach. OTOY had spent years developing OctaneRender, a physically accurate GPU rendering engine used widely in Hollywood film production, advertising, architecture and game design. The company understood, firsthand, the computational demands of high-fidelity visual content — and the high cost of centralized rendering infrastructure.

Conceptualization and Early Development (2009–2017)

Urbach first articulated the idea of a distributed GPU rendering marketplace as early as 2009, envisioning a future where idle GPU capacity around the world could be pooled and directed toward rendering work. The rise of blockchain in 2016–2017 provided the economic infrastructure — specifically, a way to issue tokens, verify work and make trustless payments — that made a decentralized rendering marketplace technically feasible.

OTOY began formally developing the blockchain-based Render Network from this foundation. The network held a token generation event in October 2017, raising capital through the sale of RNDR tokens (the original ticker before the 2023 rebrand). A private sale period followed from January to May 2018.

Public Launch and Growth (2020–2022)

The Render Network went live to the public on April 27, 2020. The timing coincided with growing mainstream interest in decentralized computing and digital content creation. The network's integration with OctaneRender — the industry-standard rendering tool — meant that professional artists and studios could access Render Network's GPU nodes without abandoning their existing software pipelines.

During the bull market of 2020–2021, the intersection of NFTs, 3D art, virtual worlds and blockchain generated enormous interest in GPU-based creative workflows. Render Network found itself well-positioned at this intersection: artists minting NFTs needed to render their 3D work, and Render Network offered a cost-effective, blockchain-native solution. The RNDR token saw significant price appreciation during this period.

Migration to Solana and the RENDER Rebrand (2023)

A pivotal development came in 2023 when the Render Network community voted to migrate the network from the Ethereum blockchain to Solana, seeking faster transaction speeds and lower fees for the high volume of micro-transactions involved in paying for rendering work. This move was accompanied by a rebranding of the token from RNDR to RENDER.

The 2023 period also brought the adoption of the Burn-and-Mint Equilibrium (BME) tokenomics model, replacing the older fixed-price structure. The Foundation was also formally established as an independent entity in the Cayman Islands, separating the network's governance from OTOY Inc. These changes collectively marked a maturation of the project from a startup experiment to a more fully formed decentralized network.

2024–2025: AI Expansion

As artificial intelligence and machine learning became one of the defining technology trends of the mid-2020s, Render Network's GPU compute infrastructure gained new relevance. The network began attracting AI developers and research groups seeking distributed GPU resources for model training and inference — a use case that extended Render Network's addressable market well beyond the creative industry.

Recent Developments

Render Network's current story sits at one of the most discussed intersections in technology: AI compute and decentralized infrastructure. As demand for GPU resources has grown dramatically — driven by the global expansion of large language models, image generation systems, video synthesis and 3D content creation — Render Network has positioned itself as a decentralized alternative to the major centralized cloud providers that currently dominate AI compute.

AI and the GPU Demand Surge

The scale of demand for GPU compute in the AI era has been well documented, with major technology companies racing to build and secure GPU capacity. Render Network's model — aggregating spare GPU capacity from distributed node operators — represents a structurally different approach: instead of building large proprietary data centers, the network grows by adding more nodes organically. As of 2026, the network has processed over 71 million frames since inception, and the combination of 3D rendering and AI workloads continues to drive usage.

RenderCon and Community Development

The Render Network has built an active creator and developer community, supported by events such as RenderCon and ongoing development on the network's open tooling. The network's compatibility with professional-grade rendering software and its increasingly streamlined onboarding for node operators have helped maintain growth in both supply (GPU capacity) and demand (rendering and AI jobs).

Burn-and-Mint Dynamics

The BME tokenomics model means that the RENDER token's supply dynamics are directly tied to network activity. High usage periods lead to more token burns, putting structural deflationary pressure on supply during periods when creative and AI workflows are most active. This design is intended to create a more sustainable and usage-linked token economy than models where a fixed supply is simply speculated upon.

Competitive Position

Render Network competes in the broader decentralized compute space alongside projects such as Akash and others, as well as indirectly with centralized cloud GPU providers. Its strongest differentiator remains its deep integration with professional 3D creative workflows and OctaneRender, which gives it a specific, professional user base that is not fully served by general-purpose decentralized compute networks.
