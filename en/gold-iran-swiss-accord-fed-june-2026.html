Gold Holds Near $3,960 as US-Iran Swiss Peace Accord Erases War Premium

Gold slid toward $3,960 by late June as a US-Iran peace agreement brokered in Switzerland removed the geopolitical risk premium that had propped prices above $4,000. A hawkish Federal Reserve and an 80% market-implied probability of a December rate hike are capping any recovery.

Published MAXGAZINE Desk · 2026-06-26

Gold prices retreated toward the $3,960 per ounce level in late June 2026, setting the metal on course for a weekly decline of approximately 5% — one of its steepest weekly drops in over a year. The primary catalyst was a diplomatic breakthrough: a US-Iran peace agreement brokered in Switzerland that, once announced, rapidly reduced the geopolitical risk premium that had supported gold above $4,000 since early in the year.

The Swiss accord also helped push oil prices back toward pre-conflict levels, reducing the inflation risk associated with energy supply disruptions and — to the extent that eased inflation lowers the case for holding gold as a store of value — removing a secondary pillar of support.

Fed hawkishness compounds the pressure

The peace accord alone does not explain the full move. The Federal Reserve's June meeting, chaired by Kevin Warsh, delivered a hold on rates but with distinctly hawkish language that shifted market expectations materially. By late June, futures markets were pricing approximately an 80% probability of a Fed rate hike in December 2026. Bank of America now forecasts three rate hikes over the course of 2026, while Deutsche Bank expects two, beginning in September.

Higher expected real rates are structurally negative for gold, which pays no yield. The US Dollar Index (DXY) had also strengthened on the hawkish Fed signals, adding additional headwinds for dollar-denominated commodities.

PCE offers temporary relief

A silver lining emerged on June 26 when US PCE (Personal Consumption Expenditures) inflation data came in broadly in line with consensus estimates. PCE is the Fed's preferred inflation gauge, and a data print that does not surprise to the upside reduced fears of an imminent emergency rate increase — helping gold rebound modestly off its session lows and defend the $3,960 area as a near-term floor.

Structural demand remains intact

Despite the near-term pressure, structural support for gold has not disappeared. Central banks have been net buyers of physical gold for 17 consecutive months, led by the People's Bank of China, whose accumulation program shows no sign of slowing. This sustained institutional demand is widely seen by analysts as providing a longer-term floor for prices even as macro headwinds build.

Oil prices returning to pre-conflict levels and gold's pullback from record highs have been welcomed by inflation-watchers, though the Fed's own communications make clear that policymakers are not yet confident they have won the battle against above-target price growth.
