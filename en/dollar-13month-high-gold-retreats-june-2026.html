Dollar Climbs to 13-Month High as Gold Slides on Hawkish Fed and Middle East Calm

The U.S. dollar index hit its highest point since May 2025 on June 27 as new Fed Chair Warsh reaffirmed hawkish rate intentions, while easing Iran-U.S. tensions stripped gold of its geopolitical risk premium, pushing the metal lower even as it trades near $4,090.

The U.S. dollar index (DXY) rose to a 13-month high on June 27, 2026, extending a streak of gains driven by the Federal Reserve's increasingly hawkish messaging under Chair Kevin Warsh. The dollar's rally has been building since Warsh's June FOMC meeting, where the committee held rates steady but revised upward its projections for the number of rate hikes expected before year-end.

Warsh reiterated on June 27 that the Fed would not yield to White House pressure to cut rates prematurely, a signal that markets had read as confirmation of tighter-for-longer monetary policy. Futures pricing now implies a roughly 62% probability of the first rate increase arriving in September, with markets pricing in a total of three hikes before the close of 2026.

Gold Loses Its Safety Bid

Gold fell 0.2% on June 27 to approximately $4,089.50 per troy ounce, continuing a weekly slide from its recent highs. The decline reflects two converging forces: a stronger dollar making the metal more expensive for non-dollar buyers, and a reduction in geopolitical risk premium as U.S.-Iran negotiations made further progress.

Oil markets told a parallel story, with crude prices retreating to pre-conflict levels as the prospect of disruptions to Persian Gulf supply lanes receded. Gold and oil had both surged in the first half of June as the Iran-U.S. standoff escalated, but the Swiss-mediated accord reported in late June progressively unwound those gains.

For dollar-denominated assets broadly, the DXY's move to a 13-month high has significant downstream effects. A stronger dollar tends to put pressure on emerging market currencies, tighten global dollar liquidity, and amplify the relative cost of dollar-denominated debt for sovereign borrowers. With rate-hike expectations now firmly priced in, the next major market catalyst is expected to be the July U.S. employment report, which will test whether the labor market remains tight enough to sustain the Fed's hawkish stance through autumn.
