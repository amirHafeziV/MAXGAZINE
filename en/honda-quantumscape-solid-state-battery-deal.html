Honda Teams Up With QuantumScape to Bring Solid-State EV Batteries to Mass Production

In a June 18 agreement, Honda and U.S. battery developer QuantumScape launched a multi-year program to industrialize anode-free lithium-metal solid-state cells — a technology that promises longer range, faster charging, and better safety than today's lithium-ion batteries.

On June 18, 2026, Honda and California-based QuantumScape announced a multi-year joint development agreement aimed at moving QuantumScape's solid-state battery technology toward mass production for electric vehicles. The program covers both cell development and the manufacturing processes needed to build the batteries at automotive scale, the companies said.

The deal follows the completion of an earlier technology evaluation agreement between the two firms, under which Honda conducted a hands-on technical study of QuantumScape's platform alongside competitive benchmarking against rival battery designs. By moving from evaluation to a formal joint development program, Honda is signaling that the technology cleared its internal review — a meaningful step in an industry where most solid-state announcements have stayed confined to the laboratory.

QuantumScape, backed for years by Volkswagen and early investor Bill Gates, builds anode-free lithium-metal cells around a proprietary ceramic separator. The architecture is designed to pack more energy into the same space than conventional lithium-ion cells, charge faster, and reduce the fire risk associated with flammable liquid electrolytes. The company has been licensing its "Cobra" separator production process to Volkswagen's battery unit PowerCo, and the Honda agreement broadens its base of automaker partners.

Why Solid-State Matters

Solid-state batteries are widely seen as the next leap for electric vehicles because they replace the liquid electrolyte at the heart of a conventional cell with a solid material. In principle that allows higher energy density — translating into more range from the same battery weight — along with shorter charging times and a lower risk of thermal runaway. The catch has long been manufacturing: producing defect-free solid-state cells consistently and cheaply at the millions-of-units scale automakers require has proven far harder than building them one at a time in a lab.

That is precisely the gap the Honda partnership targets. By pairing QuantumScape's cell chemistry with Honda's manufacturing engineering, the companies are betting they can solve the production problem rather than just the science.

A Crowded Race

Honda is far from alone. Toyota has repeatedly said it intends to commercialize solid-state batteries by the late 2020s, Nissan is building a pilot line, and Chinese manufacturers including CATL and BYD are pursuing their own solid-state and semi-solid roadmaps. The competitive pressure has intensified as EV demand growth has cooled in several Western markets, pushing automakers to chase a technology breakthrough that could reset the cost and convenience equation for buyers.

Neither Honda nor QuantumScape committed to a firm date for vehicles carrying the new cells, and solid-state timelines across the industry have slipped before. But the agreement gives QuantumScape a second major automaker relationship and gives Honda a credible path to a battery technology that could differentiate its electric lineup later this decade.
