OpenAI and Broadcom Unveil Jalapeño — a Custom AI Inference Chip to Rival Nvidia

OpenAI revealed Jalapeño on June 24 — its first custom AI inference chip, built with Broadcom in a record nine-month development cycle. The chip is designed for LLM inference, targets better performance-per-watt than current Nvidia hardware, and is set for initial deployment by year-end 2026.

Published MAXGAZINE Desk · 2026-06-25

OpenAI unveiled its first custom AI chip on June 24, 2026 — a processor called Jalapeño, developed in partnership with Broadcom and manufactured at TSMC. The announcement marks the company's first step toward silicon independence from Nvidia, which currently supplies the vast majority of the GPU compute that powers OpenAI's model training and inference infrastructure.

Jalapeño is described as an Intelligence Processor architected specifically for large language model (LLM) inference, not training. The distinction matters: inference — the process of running a trained model to generate responses — accounts for the bulk of compute costs for AI companies operating at scale. By optimizing Jalapeño around the kernels, memory movement, networking, and serving patterns specific to OpenAI's frontier models, the company aims to serve user queries more efficiently and at lower cost than is possible on general-purpose Nvidia GPUs.

Record development speed

Perhaps the most striking aspect of Jalapeño's story is how quickly it was built. OpenAI and Broadcom completed the chip from initial design to manufacturing tape-out in nine months — a timeline that engineers across the semiconductor industry describe as unusually fast for a high-performance, advanced-node ASIC. Part of that speed came from an unexpected source: OpenAI used its own AI models to accelerate parts of the design and optimization process, creating a feedback loop between AI software and AI hardware development.

The Broadcom partnership and scale ambitions

Alongside the chip reveal, OpenAI and Broadcom announced a broader strategic collaboration targeting the deployment of 10 gigawatts of OpenAI-designed AI accelerators — a scale that would represent a substantial fraction of global AI compute capacity if fully realized. The partnership also involves Celestica for board, rack, and system integration expertise.

Jalapeño is framed as the first in a multi-generation compute platform. Future chips in the family are expected to continue the performance-per-watt improvements and expand beyond inference to cover a wider range of AI workloads.

Nvidia's dominance under pressure

The move mirrors strategies already underway at Google (TPUs), Amazon (Trainium and Inferentia), and Meta (MTIA). Each of these hyperscalers has invested heavily in custom silicon to reduce dependence on Nvidia's H100 and H200 GPUs and manage AI infrastructure costs. OpenAI's entry into custom chips — funded by the company's ongoing partnership with Microsoft and its growing commercial revenue — signals that even the largest AI model company no longer treats GPU procurement as a fixed cost.

Nvidia's stock and market position remain dominant in the short term, but the long-term competitive pressure from hyperscaler and model-company custom silicon has been a recurring concern cited by analysts. Jalapeño's initial deployment is targeted for end of 2026.
