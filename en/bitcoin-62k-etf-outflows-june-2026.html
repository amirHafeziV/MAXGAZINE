Bitcoin Falls to $62,700 as Spot ETFs Post Sixth Consecutive Week of Outflows

Bitcoin traded at $62,729 on June 24, down nearly $15,000 from its May peak of $77,623, as U.S. spot Bitcoin ETFs recorded $5.94 billion in net outflows over six consecutive weeks — raising questions about whether the institutional bid that drove the 2024–2025 rally has faded.

Bitcoin traded at $62,729 on June 24, 2026, sitting approximately $14,894 below the $77,623 high it reached on May 25 and marking a 4.47% decline over the prior seven days. The fall extends a correction that began in early June after Bitcoin briefly crossed $77,000 — a level that triggered widespread profit-taking among short-term holders.

ETF Outflows: Six Weeks and $5.94 Billion

U.S. spot Bitcoin exchange-traded funds recorded net outflows in six consecutive weeks through late June 2026, with total withdrawals reaching $5.94 billion over that period. At the peak of the outflow episode in early June, funds suffered a single-week record of $3.4 billion in redemptions — the largest withdrawal since the ETFs launched in January 2024.

The outflow streak paused briefly in mid-June, when BlackRock's IBIT fund took in $57.7 million on June 12 as part of an $85.85 million daily inflow across all Bitcoin ETFs. But it subsequently resumed, underscoring that the recovery was short-lived.

Institutional positioning data adds context. In Q1 2026, institutional investors reported in regulatory 13F filings that they had reduced their spot Bitcoin ETF holdings by 17%, from 313,000 BTC to 261,000 BTC, with the dollar value of those positions falling 35% to $17.8 billion. The share of 13F filers in total Bitcoin ETF assets dropped from 24.7% to 20.8%.

What Is Driving the Selling?

Analysts point to three primary catalysts. First, rising U.S. Treasury yields have drawn capital back toward fixed-income instruments, reducing the relative appeal of non-yielding assets including Bitcoin. Second, the Federal Reserve's June meeting — the first chaired by new Fed Chair Kevin Warsh — maintained a hawkish stance, with markets now pricing roughly a 68% probability of a September rate hike. Third, macro-driven risk-off sentiment has broadly hurt speculative positions across equity and crypto markets.

The Fear & Greed Index for crypto markets stood at 24 — deep in Extreme Fear territory — as of June 24. Stablecoin supply ratios, a proxy for fresh capital entering the ecosystem, show no net new crypto inflows since late May.

The On-Chain Picture

Despite the price decline, on-chain data shows that long-term Bitcoin holders — defined as addresses holding BTC for more than 155 days without moving it — have been accumulating rather than selling. This cohort absorbed approximately 125,000 BTC between mid-May and mid-June, a pattern that has historically preceded price recoveries.

Short-term holders, by contrast, appear to be the primary sellers. The short-term holder realized price — the average cost basis for coins held fewer than 155 days — is now above the spot price, meaning recent buyers are sitting on unrealized losses.

Outlook

The divergence between weak ETF flows and strong on-chain accumulation reflects the current split in the market: institutional and short-term traders are rotating out while long-term holders absorb supply. Whether the accumulation is sufficient to establish a floor depends substantially on macroeconomic conditions in the coming weeks. A Fed pivot, a deterioration in Treasury yields, or a broad risk-on rotation could shift the balance. Until then, $62,000–$65,000 has emerged as the zone where both bulls and bears are most active.
