Pound Slides to Two-Month Low as a Cautious Bank of England Diverges From a Hawkish Fed

After the Bank of England voted 7-2 on June 18 to hold its key rate at 3.75%, sterling fell to its weakest level since early April. The move spotlights a widening policy gap between a Fed signaling more tightening and a BoE wary of weak UK growth.

The British pound dropped to its weakest level since early April after the Bank of England voted 7-2 on June 18, 2026 to keep its benchmark interest rate unchanged at 3.75%. The decision matched market expectations, but the central bank's cautious tone — and a vote split that turned slightly more hawkish, with two members now favoring a hike — left cable drifting lower against a firm U.S. dollar.

The central driver is not the level of any single rate but the gap that has opened between the world's two most-watched English-speaking central banks. The U.S. Federal Reserve, under Chair Kevin Warsh, held rates at its mid-June meeting yet published projections in which a meaningful share of policymakers still expect at least one more increase this year. Markets read that as a relatively supportive message for the dollar. The Bank of England, by contrast, paired its hold with a downgraded inflation path, trimming its projected Q4 2026 peak to 3.25% from 3.6% and citing uncertainty over the economic impact of higher energy prices tied to Middle East tensions.

A Policy U-Turn for the UK

The divergence caps a remarkable shift in expectations for UK monetary policy. At the start of 2026, markets were pricing in rate cuts; by mid-year, the debate had flipped toward whether the Bank should be raising rates instead, as an energy-driven inflation pulse complicated the outlook. The more hawkish 7-2 split reflects that tension on the Monetary Policy Committee, even as the majority chose to wait.

For now, the practical effect is a softer pound. A currency tends to weaken when its central bank is seen as less willing to defend against inflation with higher rates than its peers — and that is the perception the BoE's cautious stance has reinforced relative to the Fed.

What to Watch Next

The pound's near-term path will hinge on incoming UK data, particularly inflation and wage figures that will tell the MPC whether the energy shock is feeding into broader price pressures. A hotter reading would strengthen the case of the two dissenters and could put a floor under sterling; softer data would validate the cautious majority and keep the currency on the back foot. Traders will also watch crude oil, since cheaper energy would ease the inflation pipeline the Bank is worried about, and any further signals from the Fed, whose relative hawkishness has been the dominant force pushing the dollar higher across major pairs.
