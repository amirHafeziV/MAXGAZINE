AI's Hunger for Memory Is Quietly Raising the Price of Every Phone and PC You Buy

Memory makers Samsung, SK Hynix and Micron have redirected their factories toward the high-bandwidth chips that AI data centers crave, sending DRAM prices up roughly 90% in early 2026 and pushing phone and PC costs higher for ordinary buyers.

Published MAXGAZINE Desk · 2026-06-19

The boom in artificial intelligence is showing up in an unexpected place: the price of a new smartphone. A global shortage of memory chips — driven by data centers buying everything the industry can make — has pushed up the cost of the DRAM and storage that go into consumer electronics, and the squeeze is now reaching the shelves.

The mechanism is straightforward. AI servers depend on high-bandwidth memory (HBM), a premium, high-margin product, and the three companies that dominate memory manufacturing — Samsung Electronics, SK Hynix and Micron Technology — have shifted scarce factory capacity and capital toward it. HBM now consumes an estimated 20% or more of total global DRAM wafer output in 2026, up from near zero before the AI build-out began. Every wafer devoted to feeding a data center is a wafer not making the cheaper memory that goes into phones, laptops and game consoles.

A 90% Price Jump

The result has been a price spike that caught even seasoned analysts off guard. DRAM prices rose roughly 90% in the first quarter of 2026 compared with the final quarter of 2025, and Samsung and SK Hynix reportedly raised server DRAM prices by 60% to 70%. SK Hynix has said its HBM, DRAM and NAND capacity is essentially sold out for the year, and Micron has exited the consumer memory market entirely to concentrate on enterprise and AI customers — a move that removes a supplier from the very segment already running short.

The financial stakes are enormous. The market values of Micron, SK Hynix and Samsung each crossed the $1 trillion mark in May 2026, forming a roughly $3 trillion memory oligopoly whose pricing power now ripples across the entire electronics supply chain.

Who Pays

The cost lands on device makers and, ultimately, consumers. Apple has flagged that rising memory prices are compressing the margins on its iPhones, and it is not alone — every smartphone and PC builder buys the same chips. Research firm IDC projects the average smartphone selling price will reach a record level this year, and PC makers are warning of price increases of 15% to 20% across the industry. IDC has gone further, cautioning that entry-level phones priced under $100 could effectively disappear from the market as basic memory becomes too expensive to fit a budget device.

There is a macro dimension too. In South Korea, where memory is a pillar of the export economy, surging chip profits and large worker bonuses have drawn the attention of the central bank as a potential inflation signal — a reminder that a shortage of a single component can echo from a data center all the way into monetary policy.

Unless memory makers add capacity fast — and new fabrication lines take years — analysts expect the imbalance to persist into 2027. For buyers, the practical takeaway is simple: the AI race being fought in server halls is quietly rewriting the price tag on the devices in everyone's pocket.
