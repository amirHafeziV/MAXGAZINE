Qualcomm Bets $14 Billion on Breaking Nvidia's AI Stranglehold With RISC-V

Qualcomm is in advanced talks to acquire AI chip startup Tenstorrent for up to $10 billion — part of a $14 billion dual-acquisition strategy targeting Nvidia's dominance in AI hardware, built on open RISC-V architecture and Jim Keller's chip design expertise.

Qualcomm is negotiating to acquire Tenstorrent, a Canadian AI chip startup led by legendary chip architect Jim Keller, in a deal that would value the company at between $8 billion and $10 billion. The negotiations, first reported on June 16, 2026 and confirmed in additional reporting on June 24, position the deal as part of a larger $14 billion strategic push by Qualcomm to compete directly with Nvidia in the AI accelerator market.

The $14 billion figure reflects two parallel acquisitions. In addition to the Tenstorrent discussions, Qualcomm has confirmed a separate deal to acquire Modular, a compiler infrastructure company building open-source tooling for heterogeneous AI hardware. Together, Qualcomm says these moves form a coherent strategy: deliver AI compute hardware and the software stack needed to run it, without relying on Nvidia's CUDA ecosystem.

What Tenstorrent Brings

Tenstorrent, founded in 2016, has built a family of AI accelerators around the RISC-V open-source instruction set architecture and its proprietary Tensix core design. Its Blackhole chip — which reached general availability on April 28, 2026 — uses a fundamentally different approach to AI computation than the GPU parallelism Nvidia has refined for over two decades.

Each Tensix core packages five RISC-V processors, local on-chip SRAM, a matrix engine for tensor operations, and a vector engine — all interconnected on a chip-level mesh network. The architecture is designed to reduce the memory bottlenecks and power inefficiencies that limit GPU scaling in large-model inference workloads.

Tenstorrent raised $693 million in a Series D round in December 2024 at a valuation of more than $2.6 billion, with investors including Jeff Bezos' Bezos Expeditions and Samsung Securities. A deal at $8–10 billion would represent a roughly 3–4x markup on that valuation, reflecting how much the AI infrastructure investment climate has intensified in the 18 months since.

The Strategic Logic

Qualcomm's move reflects a growing concern among cloud providers, hyperscalers, and enterprise buyers: that Nvidia's near-monopoly in AI accelerators creates dangerous dependency and pricing power. Nvidia holds an estimated 70–80% share of the GPU accelerator market as of mid-2026, and its CUDA software ecosystem has created a switching cost that competitors have struggled to overcome.

The RISC-V angle is significant. By building on an open instruction set, Qualcomm and Tenstorrent would be able to offer cloud customers hardware that is more customizable and avoids Nvidia's proprietary toolchain lock-in. Several major hyperscalers — including Google (TPUs), Amazon (Trainium/Inferentia), and Microsoft (Maia) — have already invested billions in custom silicon for the same reason.

Qualcomm CEO Cristiano Amon has described the Tenstorrent talks as "a different kind of AI bet — one built on open foundations." No deal closing timeline has been confirmed, and both companies have declined to comment on active negotiations.

Risks and Unknowns

The acquisition faces several risks. Regulatory scrutiny is likely given the deal size and Qualcomm's existing market power in mobile chips. The talent risk is also real: Jim Keller's involvement in and commitment to the combined entity post-acquisition is unconfirmed, and his departures from previous employers (Intel, Apple, Tesla, AMD) have historically created uncertainty.

Whether Tenstorrent's Tensix architecture can realistically challenge Nvidia's CUDA-entrenched ecosystem at scale remains an open question that the market will ultimately answer.
