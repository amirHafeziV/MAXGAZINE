About

Cosmos Hub is the flagship blockchain of the Cosmos ecosystem — a network of independent, sovereign blockchains designed to communicate with one another through a protocol called the Inter-Blockchain Communication protocol, or IBC. Cosmos describes its vision as the "Internet of Blockchains": a decentralised network where separate chains can exchange data and assets without relying on centralised bridges or wrapped tokens, while each chain maintains its own governance, consensus mechanism and validator set.

ATOM is the native token of the Cosmos Hub. It is used to pay transaction fees, participate in proof-of-stake consensus as either a validator or delegator, and vote on governance proposals that determine the direction of the Hub itself. Validators who secure the network stake ATOM and earn block rewards and transaction fees, while delegators earn a portion of those rewards by bonding their ATOM to validators.

The Cosmos ecosystem is built around two core pieces of infrastructure: the Cosmos SDK, a modular framework that allows developers to build application-specific blockchains with custom logic, and the IBC protocol, which enables trustless communication between any two IBC-enabled chains. These tools have made Cosmos one of the most widely adopted blockchain development frameworks, with dozens of independent chains — including Osmosis, dYdX, Celestia, Injective and others — building on the Cosmos SDK and connected through IBC.

History

The Cosmos project was founded by Jae Kwon and Ethan Buchman, who published a white paper in 2016 outlining the vision for a network of interoperable blockchains connected by a hub-and-zone architecture. Kwon had previously developed Tendermint, a Byzantine fault-tolerant consensus algorithm, which became the foundation of the Cosmos consensus layer. The project raised approximately $17 million in an initial coin offering in April 2017, selling out in under 30 minutes in one of the most successful early blockchain fundraises.

Development of the Cosmos Hub and the associated Cosmos SDK continued over several years. The Cosmos Hub mainnet launched in March 2019 — a milestone that came after years of foundational work on the Tendermint consensus engine and the SDK. Initially, the Cosmos Hub operated as a proof-of-stake chain with ATOM used for staking and governance, but IBC was not yet live.

The IBC protocol went live in early 2021, marking the most consequential launch in the Cosmos ecosystem's history. With IBC enabled, independent chains in the Cosmos ecosystem could for the first time send tokens and data to one another without relying on centralised intermediaries. The Osmosis DEX launched shortly after, becoming the first major application to demonstrate the power of IBC by enabling token swaps across connected Cosmos chains and rapidly accumulating hundreds of millions of dollars in liquidity.

In the following years, the Cosmos ecosystem expanded dramatically as more application-specific chains — called zones — launched using the Cosmos SDK and connected via IBC. High-profile projects including dYdX (a decentralised derivatives exchange), Celestia (a modular data availability layer) and Injective (a DeFi-focused chain) all launched on or migrated to the Cosmos SDK, validating the ecosystem's thesis as a developer platform for purpose-built blockchains.

Recent Developments

The Cosmos ecosystem today represents one of the largest networks of interconnected blockchains in existence, with dozens of chains communicating through IBC and a combined total value locked that spans the entire network. While each chain in the ecosystem is sovereign and operates independently, the Cosmos Hub itself has taken on an evolving role — moving from a simple relay hub toward an emerging service platform for the chains in its orbit.

The Cosmos Hub governance process has debated and voted on significant proposals in recent years, including ATOM 2.0 — a comprehensive rethinking of ATOM's economic model that proposed new functions for the token beyond simple staking, including its role as a reserve currency for the Cosmos ecosystem. While portions of the ATOM 2.0 proposal were approved and others revised or rejected, the debate highlighted the active and opinionated nature of Cosmos governance and the ongoing conversation about how ATOM can capture value from the broader ecosystem it helped create.

Interoperability remains Cosmos's defining contribution to the blockchain landscape. IBC has become one of the most widely used cross-chain communication standards, and has been adopted beyond the original Cosmos ecosystem — with projects on other chains and platforms looking to integrate IBC support. As blockchain fragmentation continues across the industry, the Inter-Blockchain Communication protocol and the tools Cosmos pioneered position the network as an infrastructure provider for a more interconnected, multi-chain future.
