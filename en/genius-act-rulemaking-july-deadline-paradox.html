Six Agencies Must Finalize Stablecoin Rules by July 18 — But the FDIC's Comment Period Runs Until August

The GENIUS Act gave U.S. regulators exactly one year to finalize stablecoin rules—a deadline that falls on July 18, 2026. But the FDIC published its proposed rules in April with a comment period that closes August 4, creating a statutory paradox that industry lawyers say has no clean legal resolution.

One year after the GENIUS Act became law on July 18, 2025, six federal agencies face a statutory deadline to produce final stablecoin regulations. The agencies—the OCC, FDIC, NCUA, Treasury, FinCEN, and OFAC—each published proposed rules earlier this year, and all major public comment periods had formally closed by June 9, 2026, except one.

The FDIC published its GENIUS Act stablecoin proposed rule on April 10, 2026, setting a comment period that runs through August 4, 2026—seventeen days after the July 18 statutory deadline for final rules. The agency has provided no public explanation for the scheduling conflict, and legal observers say the situation has no clear statutory fix: the FDIC cannot finalize a rule before its own comment period closes without rendering the notice-and-comment process a formality.

What the Rules Require

The OCC's proposed framework sets a $5 million minimum capital floor for any new stablecoin issuer seeking federal approval, requires 1:1 liquid asset backing for all payment stablecoins, and mandates monthly reserve attestations. The FDIC's draft clarifies that stablecoin holders will not receive deposit insurance protection under any circumstances, a point intended to draw a clear line between tokenized payment instruments and insured bank deposits.

For the GENIUS Act's provisions to take effect, at least one primary federal regulator must issue final implementing rules. If that condition is met within 120 days of enactment—by November 15, 2025—the law becomes operative. If not, it defaults to taking effect 18 months after enactment, meaning January 18, 2027.

Industry Impact

For stablecoin issuers, the regulatory uncertainty has a practical cost. Companies including Circle, Paxos, and several large bank stablecoin programs have been holding expansion decisions pending the final OCC and FDIC frameworks. The Tether-Ledn gold-loan announcement and the growing use of USDT and USAT in institutional lending reflect an industry proceeding in the absence of complete regulatory clarity.

Legal analysts at Sullivan & Cromwell and Chapman and Cutler note that regulators have some flexibility to issue interim final rules or no-action guidance that addresses the gap. Whether the FDIC chooses to accelerate its process, issue an interim rule, or simply acknowledge a delay will likely be one of the more consequential regulatory decisions in U.S. crypto policy before year-end.
