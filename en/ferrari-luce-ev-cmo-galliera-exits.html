Ferrari Replaces Marketing Chief After Luce EV Debut Wiped Billions Off Its Stock

Ferrari has appointed former BMW Italy boss Massimiliano Di Silvestre as its new Chief Marketing & Commercial Officer, effective July 1, 2026 — replacing Enrico Galliera, who spent 16 years at the brand and oversaw the controversial reveal of the Luce, Ferrari's first electric car, which sent shares down more than 8% in May.

Ferrari announced on June 23, 2026, the appointment of Massimiliano Di Silvestre as its Chief Marketing & Commercial Officer, effective July 1, 2026. Di Silvestre, previously CEO of BMW Italy, succeeds Enrico Galliera, who spent more than sixteen years at Ferrari and announced his departure to "embark on a new chapter" in his professional career.

The timing is impossible to ignore. The announcement comes exactly one month after the rocky public debut of the Luce — Ferrari's first fully electric car — which triggered an immediate sell-off in Ferrari's Milan-listed shares, sending them down more than 8% on the day of the reveal in late May 2026. The stock decline erased billions of euros in market capitalization in a single session.

What Went Wrong With the Luce Reveal?

Ferrari CEO Benedetto Vigna has defended the Luce and its pricing, but the market reaction reflected deep tension between Ferrari's heritage positioning and the practical challenges of transitioning to electric. The Luce is priced well above Ferrari's existing model range, placing it in a segment where it faces not only performance expectations from traditional Ferrari buyers but also new luxury EV competition from Rimac, Lotus, and emerging ultra-luxury Chinese brands.

Coverage in the financial and automotive press pointed to several concerns: the Luce's range figures were seen as underwhelming relative to comparable luxury EV offerings; the charging infrastructure for its multi-megawatt fast-charging specification remains limited even in key European markets; and some critics argued that the reveal itself — the brand narrative, the event staging, the price announcement sequence — misjudged the expectations of both Ferrari's core collectors and the broader media.

Ferrari said Galliera had shared his decision to leave "some time ago" and had agreed to remain through the Luce launch. The company characterized the transition as planned, not reactive.

Who Is Di Silvestre?

Massimiliano Di Silvestre served as President and CEO of BMW Italia, building a track record of premium brand positioning in a market where BMW's lineup competes directly with Ferrari's entry-level models. His background is in luxury automotive marketing across the European market, and he has been involved in BMW's EV transition communications — including the iX, i5, and i7 launches in Italy.

The appointment of a BMW executive to lead Ferrari's commercial strategy carries symbolic weight: BMW has executed one of the automotive industry's more successful premium-to-electric transitions, and Di Silvestre's experience navigating that shift may be exactly what Ferrari needs as it manages the Luce's market reception.

The Broader Stakes

Ferrari's entry into the EV market is strategically significant beyond the Luce itself. The company has committed to a hybrid and electric future, with its electrified model lineup set to account for a substantial majority of sales by the end of the decade. How it manages the brand narrative — convincing buyers that an electric Ferrari is still unmistakably a Ferrari — will determine whether the Luce is remembered as a stumble or a turning point.

The leadership change sends a signal that Ferrari's board is not satisfied with how the Luce story has been told so far. Di Silvestre's first challenge will be to reframe the narrative before the car enters full production and customer deliveries in 2027.
