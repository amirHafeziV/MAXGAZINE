Ethereum Foundation Cuts 54 Jobs and 40% of Its Budget in a Sweeping Institutional Reset

The nonprofit that steers Ethereum's development is shedding a fifth of its workforce and shifting to an endowment model — changes accelerated by a wave of nine senior departures since January and a steep decline in the value of its ETH treasury.

The Ethereum Foundation confirmed on June 23 that it is laying off 54 employees — roughly one in five of its approximately 270-person workforce — while reducing its annual budget by approximately 40%. The move marks one of the most sweeping institutional changes in the foundation's decade-long history.

The restructuring follows months of leadership turbulence. Since January 2026, nine senior figures have departed the foundation, most recently Co-Executive Director Hsiao-Wei Wang, who resigned in early June. That turnover has added pressure on the remaining leadership to demonstrate a credible path forward at a time when Ethereum's competitive position is under scrutiny from faster-moving rivals.

A Shift to an Endowment Model

Under the new operating approach, the foundation intends to cut its annual spending rate from roughly 15% of its treasury to around 5% by 2030. That shift — from active institutional spending to an endowment-style model — is designed to extend the organization's financial runway indefinitely, rather than relying on periodic ETH sales to fund operations. Severance packages for departing employees include at least one month's salary per year of service, a retirement payment, and access to a support fund that includes career coaching and ecosystem placement assistance.

The foundation is also reorganizing its remaining work around five new clusters: protocol, access, user, community, and institutional layers. Several existing programs are being wound down, including the Privacy and Scaling Explorations (PSE) unit, which had been responsible for applied zero-knowledge research. Devcon — Ethereum's flagship annual conference — is expected to run in a smaller format going forward. Research work will rely more heavily on AI-assisted formal verification tooling to reduce costs.

The Financial Pressure Behind the Cuts

The cost pressures are partly driven by asset prices. ETH has been trading near $1,580 in June 2026 — a steep decline from highs above $4,000 in late 2024. Since the foundation holds the bulk of its treasury in ETH, that depreciation has materially shrunk the real size of its endowment, making the previous spending pace difficult to sustain without accelerating token sales.

Whether this restructuring strengthens or weakens Ethereum's development capacity remains actively debated within the community. Supporters argue that a leaner, more focused foundation is better positioned to coordinate protocol work without unnecessary overhead. Critics warn that shrinking the team responsible for coordinating client development, research funding, and ecosystem grants during a competitive moment — when rivals such as Solana are rolling out major protocol upgrades aimed at sub-second finality — could slow Ethereum's ability to respond to new technical and market challenges.
