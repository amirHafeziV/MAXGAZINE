Ferrari's $640,000 Luce Is Its First EV — and It Sent the Stock Down 8%. Here's Why the Market Is Wrong to Panic.

Unveiled in Rome on May 26, the Luce is Ferrari's first fully electric car — a four-motor, five-seat grand tourer designed by Jony Ive. Investors hated it. Analysts say they are missing the point entirely.

Ferrari unveiled the Luce — Italian for "light" — at a gala event in Rome on May 26, 2026. What followed was a textbook case of the market misreading a strategic move.

The Luce is priced at €550,000 (approximately $640,000), making it among the most expensive electric vehicles ever produced. It was co-designed with LoveFrom, the creative collective led by former Apple chief designer Jony Ive. The result is a four-motor grand tourer with 772 kW of peak power, 0–100 km/h in 2.5 seconds, and a top speed exceeding 310 km/h. It is also Ferrari's first five-seater — a deliberate attempt to expand the brand into a new family of buyer.

The Market Reaction

Ferrari's Milan-listed stock fell 8.4% the day after the unveiling; shares in New York dropped over 5%. The reaction appeared driven by two concerns: the car's design — which the internet immediately compared to a Nissan — and anxiety that Ferrari was abandoning its sports-car roots for a luxury family vehicle.

Analysts at Citi quickly stepped in to say investors should not be "overly concerned." The broader context: Ferrari's stock had already declined roughly 30% year-to-date before the Luce announcement, reflecting sector-wide headwinds for luxury goods and auto stocks, not a Ferrari-specific problem.

Why the Long View Matters

Ferrari has long been valued not as a car company but as a luxury goods company that happens to make cars — similar in category to Hermès rather than Volkswagen. In that frame, the Luce is not Ferrari abandoning its identity; it is Ferrari extending its brand upward into an even more exclusive tier.

At €550,000, the Luce competes with nobody. Lamborghini and Porsche have each scaled back EV timelines citing weak demand, creating a window for Ferrari to claim the ultra-premium electric segment with almost no direct competition. Every Luce sold will carry margins that make the average sports car look modest.

The Jony Ive collaboration is also worth noting. Ive's design philosophy — spare, precise, premium materials — has historically commanded a price premium when applied to products (see: every iPhone from 2007 to 2019). Applying it to a €550,000 car is either brilliant positioning or a profound miscalculation. The market voted miscalculation on day one; the answer will not be known until deliveries begin in Q4 2026.

The Bigger EV Landscape

Ferrari's move comes as the global EV market faces uneven growth. BloombergNEF projects 27% of global car sales will be electric in 2026, but US EV sales are set to fall 19% this year following the rollback of federal incentives. China, with 63% of global EV sales, is growing but crowded.

For Ferrari, none of that matters. The Luce is not sold to the mass market. It is sold to a few hundred buyers globally who have the means and desire to own the first Ferrari EV. In that sense, the macro EV slowdown is noise; the luxury client waiting list is the only data point that matters.
