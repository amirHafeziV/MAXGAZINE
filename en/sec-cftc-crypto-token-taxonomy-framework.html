How US Regulators Are Sorting Crypto Into Five Boxes — and Why It Matters for Every Token

A joint SEC-CFTC interpretation issued in March is reshaping US crypto oversight by classifying digital assets into five categories. Most are not securities on their own — but how a token is sold can still pull it under securities law.

Published MAXGAZINE Desk · 2026-06-19
AD SPACEIn-article bannerBook this spot →

After years of regulation by enforcement, the United States is finally giving the crypto industry something it has long demanded: a written map of what counts as what. A joint interpretation issued by the Securities and Exchange Commission on March 17, 2026, and endorsed by the Commodity Futures Trading Commission, lays out a coherent taxonomy that sorts digital assets into clear categories — and it is already changing how projects launch, list, and market their tokens.

The five categories

The interpretation groups crypto assets into digital commodities, digital collectibles, digital tools, stablecoins, and digital securities. The crucial distinction is that digital commodities, collectibles, tools, and certain stablecoins are not, in themselves, securities. That single line removes a cloud of legal uncertainty that has hung over thousands of tokens and the exchanges that list them, and it hands the CFTC a clearer remit over the assets treated as commodities.

The catch: how you sell it still matters

The relief is real but conditional. The regulators were explicit that an asset which is not a security on its own can still be offered and sold as part of an "investment contract" — and that investment contract is itself a security. In practice, this means the way a token is marketed, the promises made to buyers, and the structure of a fundraising round can pull an otherwise non-security token under securities law. The classification therefore turns on conduct, not just on the technology of the token.

Why the CFTC role is expanding

By confirming that staff will administer the Commodity Exchange Act consistently with the SEC's reading, the CFTC signalled a more central role in day-to-day crypto oversight. The agency is separately developing guidance on when intermediary registration requirements apply to digital wallets and decentralized-finance applications — among the hardest questions in the entire framework, because DeFi protocols often have no obvious operator to register in the first place.

What to watch next

The interpretation is guidance, not legislation, and it sits alongside a market-structure bill still moving through Congress. The open questions for the rest of 2026 are how aggressively the two agencies apply the investment-contract test to new token sales, how the promised DeFi guidance treats non-custodial software, and whether Congress codifies the taxonomy into statute so that a future administration cannot simply reverse it. For founders and exchanges, the practical takeaway is immediate: the label on a token now depends as much on how it is sold as on what it is.
