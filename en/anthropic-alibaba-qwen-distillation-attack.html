Anthropic Accuses Alibaba's Qwen Lab of Largest-Ever Claude Distillation Attack Using 25,000 Fake Accounts

Anthropic sent a letter to the US Senate Banking Committee on June 24, 2026 alleging that operators linked to Alibaba's Qwen AI lab ran a 44-day campaign generating 28.8 million interactions with Claude models through nearly 25,000 fraudulent accounts — a practice it called adversarial distillation and the largest known attack of its kind on a US AI system.

In a letter dated June 10, 2026, Anthropic alleged that operators affiliated with Alibaba Group's Qwen AI lab conducted what it described as the largest-ever known distillation campaign against a US AI company. The campaign ran from April 22 to June 5, 2026, and involved close to 25,000 fraudulent accounts generating approximately 28.8 million exchanges with Claude models — with a specific focus on Claude's advanced software engineering and agentic reasoning capabilities.

Anthropic sent the letter to Senators Tim Scott (R-SC) and Elizabeth Warren (D-MA), the chair and ranking member of the Senate Banking Committee, respectively. The letter was also circulated to White House officials.

What Is Distillation and Why Is It Controversial?

AI distillation is the practice of training a smaller or less capable model using outputs generated by a more powerful system. When done through legitimate research partnerships, it is standard in the industry. When conducted through fraudulent accounts that violate a provider's terms of service, Anthropic and other AI labs classify it as adversarial distillation or model theft.

The concern is economic and strategic: adversarial distillation allows a competitor to approximate the capabilities of a frontier model at a fraction of the research and training cost. Crucially, the resulting model may also lack the original safety training and alignment work — producing a capable system without the guardrails.

Chinese AI labs, including Alibaba's Qwen, have released openly available models that have narrowed the gap with US frontier systems over the past year. Critics of Qwen have alleged its rapid capability gains were partially accelerated through distillation from US models, a charge Alibaba has denied.

The Scale of the Alleged Campaign

According to Anthropic's letter, the campaign was not casual or exploratory. The 25,000 fraudulent accounts ran a systematic pattern of queries specifically targeting Claude's highest-value capabilities: multi-step coding tasks, autonomous agent workflows, and complex reasoning chains. The volume — 28.8 million exchanges over 44 days — averages approximately 655,000 interactions per day.

Anthropic estimates that replicating this scale of capability extraction through legitimate API usage would have cost millions of dollars at standard pricing. The fraudulent accounts reduced this cost to near-zero by circumventing payment and identity controls.

Congressional and Administration Response

Senators Bill Hagerty (R-TN) and Andy Kim (D-NJ) are working to attach an amendment to pending defense legislation that would allow the United States to blacklist or sanction entities determined to have conducted adversarial distillation against US AI companies. The amendment would give the Commerce Department new authority over export controls specifically tailored to AI model capabilities.

The White House has been briefed on the Anthropic letter. US export controls already cover advanced semiconductors used in AI training, but no legislation currently regulates the transfer of AI model capabilities through distillation.

Alibaba's Response

Alibaba has not publicly responded to Anthropic's specific allegations as of late June 2026. The company has previously denied that its Qwen AI research relied on outputs from competing US models.

Broader Implications

The Anthropic letter is part of a broader pattern of US AI companies seeking legislative protection for their models. It arrives as Congress debates the AI SAFETY Act, an GENIUS Act equivalent for model security, and as the US Commerce Department is updating its AI export control framework. If enacted, anti-distillation sanctions would represent the first time US law explicitly treated AI model capabilities as export-controlled technology.
