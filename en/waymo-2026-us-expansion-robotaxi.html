Waymo Now Operates in 11 US Cities and Is Targeting 20 More — Tesla's Robotaxi Still Amounts to Four Cars in Two Cities

Waymo opened full commercial service in Nashville in June and plans expansion to San Diego, Detroit, and Las Vegas later in 2026 — all while targeting more than 20 cities worldwide including Tokyo and London. The autonomous vehicle race has a clear early leader, and it is not Tesla.

Alphabet's autonomous vehicle division Waymo has now established commercial robotaxi service in eleven American cities and is preparing its most ambitious expansion to date, with plans to enter San Diego, Detroit, and Las Vegas before the end of 2026 — and internationally, Tokyo and London before the end of the decade.

The company opened full commercial service in Nashville in late April 2026, with anyone in the service area able to request a ride immediately through the Waymo app without a waiting list. By June 2026, tens of thousands of Nashville residents had registered for the service, according to the company. A Lyft integration allowing rides to be booked through the Lyft app is expected later this year.

The Scale Gap With Tesla

The contrast with Tesla's autonomous vehicle program is stark. Tesla launched its own robotaxi service in Austin, Texas in June 2025, initially with human safety monitors in the vehicle, and transitioned to unsupervised operation in January 2026. However, a Reuters investigation published in June 2026 found that the Austin fleet amounts to approximately four vehicles across two cities, with users reporting hours-long wait times and unusual routing behaviors.

The divergence between the two programs reflects fundamentally different technical approaches. Waymo uses a combination of lidar sensors, cameras, radar, and high-definition mapping that is built before each new launch, which requires substantial up-front preparation but produces reliable performance within defined geofences. Tesla relies entirely on cameras and a vision-based neural network approach that the company argues should, in theory, generalize more easily to new locations without requiring pre-mapped road data.

In practice, Waymo's approach has produced a commercially operational, safety-record-carrying robotaxi service in eleven cities. Tesla's approach has produced a very small, geofenced fleet that is not available to general public bookings in most of its claimed service areas.

Waymo's 2026 Expansion in Numbers

Waymo reached an annualized pace of approximately 14 million fully autonomous trips in 2025 and completed 500,000 weekly rides by early 2026, tripling its volume from 2024. The company is now in the early stages of its most geographically ambitious phase: Nashville adds to existing operations in San Francisco, Los Angeles, Phoenix, Dallas, Houston, San Antonio, Orlando, Atlanta, Miami, and Washington D.C. San Diego, Detroit, and Las Vegas are confirmed for later in 2026, and Tokyo and London launches are planned for the next several years.

The commercial scale Waymo has now achieved is significant because it crosses a threshold above which autonomous vehicle economics begin to look viable. At 500,000 weekly rides, the fixed costs of fleet management, maintenance, and mapping are spread across a growing revenue base, and the system accumulates the edge-case driving data needed to make the technology increasingly reliable in new environments.

What the Industry Is Watching

The more important question for the automotive industry in late 2026 is not who leads today but whether either Tesla or Waymo — or a Chinese competitor such as Baidu's Apollo or Pony.AI — will be the first to demonstrate that fully autonomous commercial service can be profitable at scale without continuous heavy subsidy. Waymo's Alphabet parent has invested more than $16 billion in the company over two decades. The path to profitability remains the central unresolved question, but the operational distance between Waymo and every other player has never been wider.
