Global Automakers Have Now Written Off $70 Billion as EV Plans Collapse

Ford, GM, Stellantis, and Honda have booked more than $70 billion in combined EV-related writedowns since late 2025, cancelling models and shifting investment back to hybrids and combustion vehicles.

The global automotive industry's electric vehicle pivot has turned into one of the costliest strategic reversals in corporate history. Across Ford, General Motors, Stellantis, and Honda, combined EV-related writedowns have now surpassed $70 billion — a figure that reflects cancelled models, stranded factory investments, and battery supply agreements that no longer make financial sense at current demand levels.

The Writedowns in Detail

Stellantis led the way with a €22.2 billion ($26 billion) restructuring charge disclosed in February 2026, covering EV factory capacity that is now running well below planned utilisation. Ford followed with a $19.5 billion write-down announced in December 2025, tied to the cancellation of several electric models — most notably the F-150 Lightning electric pickup — and a decision to redirect capital toward its truck and hybrid programmes. Honda has disclosed a restructuring impact of approximately ¥2.5 trillion ($15.7 billion) in connection with cancelling three EV models intended for the U.S. market: the Honda 0 SUV, the Honda 0 Saloon, and the Acura RSX.

General Motors has also booked significant EV-related charges, though it has spread its disclosures across multiple quarters.

Why the Reversal

Three policy changes accelerated what had already been a demand slowdown. The U.S. federal EV tax credit for consumers, which had subsidised EV purchases since 2022, expired at the end of the third quarter of 2025. In December 2025, the Trump administration formally rolled back Biden-era fuel economy standards that had effectively mandated a rising share of EV sales. And also in December 2025, the European Union cancelled its 2035 ICE vehicle ban, removing a hard deadline that had forced manufacturers to commit to full electrification regardless of market conditions.

Without those mandates and subsidies, demand for EVs — particularly in the mid-to-large segment in the U.S. and Europe — has fallen significantly short of what automakers had projected when they committed tens of billions of dollars in factory retooling and battery supply agreements between 2021 and 2024.

Who Is Still Investing

Not all manufacturers are in retreat. Chinese automakers, led by BYD, continue to expand aggressively and have benefited from a domestic market that still incentivises EV adoption. Tesla, despite its own challenges, remains the global EV market leader. And in the premium segment, brands such as BMW and Mercedes-Benz are continuing their electrification programmes, though at a more measured pace than initially announced.

The pattern that is emerging in 2026 is a two-track industry: a Chinese-led segment where EVs are now the mainstream product, and a Western-led segment that has largely pivoted to hybrids as a transition technology, buying time until battery costs fall further and charging infrastructure matures.

What Comes Next

Analysts expect the wave of writedowns to ease in the second half of 2026 as manufacturers complete their portfolio realignments. The longer-term question is whether the regulatory environment will stabilise enough to allow automakers to commit to new EV investment cycles — or whether the policy uncertainty of the past 12 months will continue to make long-term planning difficult.
