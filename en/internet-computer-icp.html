About

The Internet Computer (ICP) is a blockchain network created by the DFINITY Foundation with the stated aim of extending the public internet with decentralized computing capacity, allowing developers to build and deploy software directly on the blockchain rather than on traditional cloud servers owned by companies like Amazon, Google or Microsoft. The project describes itself as a "World Computer" — a global, decentralized platform where web applications, DeFi protocols, NFT marketplaces, social platforms and enterprise systems can be hosted entirely on-chain.

The fundamental technical building block of the Internet Computer is the "canister," which is an evolved form of smart contract. Unlike Ethereum's smart contracts, which are limited in size and computation, canisters on the Internet Computer can store large amounts of data, run complex software, and serve web content directly to users' browsers without any centralized servers in between. This makes it theoretically possible to build a full-stack application — frontend, backend, and database — entirely on the blockchain.

The network uses a novel consensus mechanism and cryptographic approach called Chain Key Technology, which involves a system of threshold cryptography enabling the entire network to be governed by a single public key. This allows the Internet Computer to scale horizontally by adding new subnets (groups of nodes) without compromising security or decentralization. Governance of the network is handled by the Network Nervous System (NNS), an on-chain algorithmic governance system where ICP token holders stake their tokens to participate in voting on upgrades and decisions.

The ICP token serves multiple roles: it is used to pay for computation and storage on the network (which is burned as fees), it can be staked in the NNS to participate in governance and earn voting rewards, and it can be converted into "cycles" — the unit of computation fuel that powers canister smart contracts on the network.

History

DFINITY was founded by Dominic Williams, a British entrepreneur and computer scientist who had been thinking about ideas for a decentralized internet since around 2014. Williams began formalizing the concept of a "World Computer" and established DFINITY as a nonprofit foundation in 2016. The project attracted significant attention and funding in the crypto community early on, raising roughly $102 million through private token sales before mainnet launch.

DFINITY spent several years in research and development, publishing academic papers and building out the cryptographic and distributed systems infrastructure required for its ambitious design. The project attracted top researchers and engineers from universities and technology companies globally, positioning itself as a technically serious research-driven project rather than a quick token issuance.

The Internet Computer's mainnet launched publicly on May 10, 2021, in what DFINITY called a "Genesis" event. The ICP token became publicly tradeable at the same time and initially traded at very high valuations — reaching prices well above $400 and briefly approaching $750 — as the launch generated enormous initial excitement. However, the token's price fell dramatically in the months that followed, dropping over 95% from its peak, which drew significant criticism and scrutiny from the broader crypto community about tokenomics, early investor unlocks, and the project's initial circulating supply.

Despite the turbulent price history, DFINITY continued building. The team delivered a succession of technical milestones, including Bitcoin integration in 2022 — allowing smart contracts on the Internet Computer to hold and transact real Bitcoin without bridges — and Ethereum integration, enabling cross-chain interoperability. The network also grew its developer community steadily throughout this period.

Recent Developments

The Internet Computer today hosts a growing ecosystem of on-chain applications across DeFi, social media, gaming and enterprise use cases. Projects built on the network include decentralized social platforms, NFT marketplaces, DeFi exchanges, and developer tools, all running directly in canister smart contracts without any off-chain components. The NNS continues to govern the network's upgrades and parameters through on-chain voting, making the Internet Computer one of the more actively governed blockchain networks in the space.

A major technical feature that has continued to expand is the "Chain Fusion" initiative — DFINITY's umbrella term for its work connecting the Internet Computer with Bitcoin, Ethereum and other blockchains natively, without relying on bridges or wrapped tokens. This allows ICP canisters to sign and submit transactions on Bitcoin's network and interact with Ethereum smart contracts, positioning the Internet Computer as a potential orchestration layer for multi-chain applications.

On the economic side, DFINITY has worked to grow the developer ecosystem through grants, hackathons and educational programs. The ICP token's deflationary pressure comes from its use as computation fuel via the cycle conversion mechanism, which permanently burns ICP as canisters run computation.

The Internet Computer remains a project that asks the industry to reconsider some fundamental assumptions about how blockchain applications are built and hosted. Whether its vision of a fully on-chain internet achieves mainstream developer and user adoption will be one of the more consequential stories in the longer arc of blockchain technology.
