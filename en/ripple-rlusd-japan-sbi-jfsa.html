Ripple and SBI Launch RLUSD in Japan After JFSA Approval

Ripple's dollar stablecoin became Japan's first regulated foreign-currency digital payment instrument on June 24, clearing a JFSA hurdle — but an Ethereum-only setup and a ¥1 million transaction cap reveal the early limits of even a well-regulated launch.

Published MAXGAZINE Desk · 2026-06-24
AD SPACEIn-article bannerBook this spot →

Ripple and SBI Group officially launched RLUSD, a U.S. dollar-backed stablecoin, in Japan on June 24, 2026, following regulatory approval from the Japan Financial Services Agency (JFSA). The token has been classified as a "Type 4" electronic payment instrument under Japanese law — a designation created specifically to accommodate foreign-currency-denominated stablecoins — and is now available to institutions and retail users through SBI VC Trade, the regulated exchange arm of Japan's SBI Group.

RLUSD has accumulated a $1.7 billion market capitalization since its global launch in late 2024. It is issued by Standard Custody & Trust Company, a New York-chartered Ripple subsidiary, with each token backed 1:1 by U.S. dollar deposits and short-term U.S. Treasury bills — placing it in the same collateral tier as USDC and USDT.

Ethereum, Not XRP Ledger

A notable technical detail of the Japan launch: SBI VC Trade is processing only Ethereum-based RLUSD tokens, not those on Ripple's own XRP Ledger. Ripple has positioned RLUSD as a multi-chain asset, but the practical infrastructure for the Japanese market runs on Ethereum — a reflection of that chain's deeper DeFi liquidity and broader institutional wallet support in the Asia-Pacific region.

Regulatory constraints remain significant in the initial phase. Japanese rules cap individual RLUSD transactions at one million yen — roughly $6,200 at June 2026 exchange rates — limiting the instrument to retail-sized transfers for now. Ripple and SBI indicated they expect that ceiling to rise as regulators build confidence in the framework and as market participants demonstrate compliance.

Japan's Stablecoin-Forward Posture

Japan has been one of the most systematically crypto-forward G7 economies, having built a formal stablecoin licensing regime in 2023 and expanded it since. The JFSA approval of RLUSD marks the first foreign-currency stablecoin to clear this regulatory hurdle, giving Ripple a beachhead in one of the world's largest cross-border payment corridors.

Japan sends substantial remittance and trade-settlement flows to Southeast Asia, the United States, and the Middle East — markets where Ripple's existing On-Demand Liquidity (ODL) rails already operate. The RLUSD launch strengthens Ripple's position in those corridors by providing a regulated dollar-denominated settlement layer within the Japanese financial system.

The Global Stablecoin Moment

The broader context is a convergence of stablecoin regulation across major economies. The U.S. GENIUS Act, signed into law in early June 2026, established the first federal framework for dollar stablecoins, effectively legitimizing the asset class for American institutions. The JFSA approval of RLUSD arrives just weeks later, suggesting regulators in G7 nations are moving toward a shared framework: compliant stablecoins are infrastructure, not speculation.

Ripple CEO Brad Garlinghouse said in a statement that RLUSD's Japan approval "validates that compliant stablecoins can operate within traditional financial systems rather than around them." For SBI, the partnership extends an already deep relationship with Ripple that has run the ODL payments corridor between Japan and Southeast Asia for several years.
