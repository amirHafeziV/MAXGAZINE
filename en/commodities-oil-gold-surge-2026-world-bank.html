Oil and Gold Are Surging Together in 2026 — and That Combination Usually Spells Trouble

The World Bank expects global commodity prices to rise for the first time since 2022, led by an energy shock and record demand for safe-haven metals — a rare dual rally that points to both inflation and fear.

Published MAXGAZINE Desk · 2026-06-19
AD SPACEIn-article bannerBook this spot →

Two of the world's most-watched commodities are climbing at once, and history suggests that is rarely a calm signal. In its April 2026 Commodity Markets Outlook, the World Bank forecast that global commodity prices will rise about 16% this year — the first annual increase since 2022 — driven mainly by supply disruptions in the Middle East. Energy prices are projected to climb 24%, metals and minerals 17%, and precious metals a striking 42%, with both base and precious metals on course for all-time highs.

An energy shock on the oil side

The oil move has been sharp. Brent crude rose roughly 20% in the first two months of 2026 and pushed above $100 a barrel in mid-March amid severe disruptions to shipments through the Strait of Hormuz, a chokepoint that handles a large share of the world's seaborne crude. By the end of March, Brent had surged about 65% — a jump of roughly $46 a barrel and its largest monthly increase on record. The World Bank still expects Brent to average $86 in 2026 and $70 in 2027, but only if the most acute phase of supply disruptions eases and Middle East exports recover toward pre-conflict levels later in the year.

A safe-haven bid on the gold side

Gold has been climbing for very different reasons. Precious metals have continued to trade at or near record highs, supported by safe-haven demand, sustained central-bank buying, and persistent geopolitical and monetary uncertainty. The World Bank's 42% projected gain for precious metals reflects how broad that demand has become as investors seek protection.

Why the combination matters

Oil and gold rising together is unusual, and it tends to send a specific message. Oil is being lifted by war and supply fears, while gold is being lifted by the search for safety and longer-term concerns about inflation and money. When both move up at the same time, markets are often signalling a difficult mix of inflation risk, geopolitical stress, and economic uncertainty all at once.

The macro ripple effects

Higher energy costs feed directly into inflation, complicating the job of central banks that are already wary of cutting rates. That backdrop helps explain why several major central banks have leaned hawkish or raised rates in 2026 rather than easing. For households and businesses across MAXGAZINE's markets, the practical takeaway is the same everywhere: fuel and imported goods cost more, and the value of holding hard assets is back in the spotlight.
