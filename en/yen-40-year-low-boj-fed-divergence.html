The Yen Sinks Near a 40-Year Low Even After the Bank of Japan Raised Rates

The Japanese currency weakened to around 161.5 per dollar on June 22, near its lowest since 1986, as a widening gap between Tokyo's slow tightening and a hawkish Federal Reserve overwhelmed repeated official warnings.

The Japanese yen is testing levels not seen in nearly four decades, and the unusual part is that it is doing so despite the Bank of Japan actively raising interest rates. On June 22, 2026 the currency weakened to around 161.5 per US dollar, hovering near its lowest point since 1986, as repeated verbal interventions from Tokyo failed to halt the slide.

A rate hike that didn't help

The weakness is striking because it runs against the textbook. The Bank of Japan has been normalizing policy, including a 25-basis-point increase that lifted its benchmark rate to 1% the previous week. In normal conditions, higher rates support a currency. The yen has fallen anyway, because what matters to traders is not Japan's policy in isolation but the gap between Japanese and US interest rates — and that gap remains wide.

The Fed is the other half of the story

That divergence has been reinforced from Washington. The Federal Reserve recently left its own rates unchanged while signalling growing support for additional hikes later in the year, and the dollar climbed to its strongest level in more than a year. With US yields holding high and Japanese yields rising only slowly, capital continues to favour the dollar — the engine behind the long-running "carry trade," in which investors borrow cheaply in yen to buy higher-yielding assets elsewhere.

Verbal intervention is wearing thin

Japanese officials have leaned on words to slow the decline, warning markets against excessive moves. So far those warnings have had limited effect, raising the question of whether Tokyo will move from rhetoric to direct currency-market intervention, as it has during past bouts of yen weakness. Each failed verbal warning makes the next one less credible, which is part of why the currency keeps drifting lower.

What is at stake

A weak yen is a double-edged sword for the world's third-largest economy. It flatters Japanese exporters and the country's stock market by making goods cheaper abroad, but it raises the cost of imported energy and food, squeezing households at a time when inflation is already a concern. For global markets, a yen near multi-decade lows keeps the carry trade alive and the risk of a sudden, disorderly unwind on the table — the kind of move that has rattled markets before. The next signals to watch are whether the Bank of Japan accelerates its tightening and whether Tokyo finally intervenes directly to defend the currency.
