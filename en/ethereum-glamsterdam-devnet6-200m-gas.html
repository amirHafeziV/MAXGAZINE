Ethereum's Glamsterdam Upgrade Enters Final Devnet Phase Targeting 200M Gas Limit

Ethereum developers have launched the final multi-client devnet for the Glamsterdam hard fork, the network's most significant overhaul since the Merge, with a target gas limit of 200 million per block and a mainnet launch window targeting the second half of 2026.

Ethereum developers confirmed in late June 2026 that Glamsterdam's final devnet — devnet-6 — is live across multiple client implementations, marking the last major engineering milestone before public testnet launches and security reviews begin. The six-client devnet tests the full slate of Ethereum Improvement Proposals slated for the upgrade.

What Glamsterdam Changes

Glamsterdam is headlined by two transformative features: Enshrined Proposer-Builder Separation (EIP-7732, known as ePBS) and Block-Level Access Lists (EIP-7928). Together, these changes move block building into the protocol layer and lay the groundwork for parallel transaction execution — the mechanism developers believe can push Ethereum toward 10,000 transactions per second.

The most immediately visible change is the gas limit increase. Ethereum's gas ceiling, which governs how many operations can occur in a single block, is slated to climb from roughly 60 million to 200 million — a 3.3x increase. Analysts at major staking providers project the upgrade will cut average fees by roughly 78% once the new gas capacity takes effect.

A third key feature, ePBS, restructures how validators and specialized block builders interact. Currently, block builders operate outside the protocol through off-chain agreements (MEV-Boost). Glamsterdam brings this relationship on-chain, which developers argue makes the network more transparent and censorship-resistant.

Timeline and Risks

The final devnet marks the beginning of a multi-month testing window. Ethereum's recent upgrade history — Pectra in May 2025 and Dencun in March 2024 — required roughly two to four months of public-testnet testing before mainnet deployment. If devnet-6 and subsequent public testnets proceed cleanly, the H2 2026 target is achievable, with an optimistic internal window around September to October.

However, Glamsterdam's meta specification (EIP-7773) remains in Draft status, and the sheer scope of the changes carries meaningful execution risk. A failed devnet or unexpected consensus bug at the public-testnet stage would likely push mainnet to Q1 2027.

What It Means for ETH Holders

Fee reduction is the clearest near-term impact for retail users. A 200 million gas limit, combined with EIP-4844 blob fees introduced during Dencun, would make Ethereum layer-2 rollups dramatically cheaper and draw more activity to the base layer. Staking yields, which currently range from 3% to 5% annually, are unlikely to shift materially from the upgrade itself, though higher on-chain activity tends to increase tip fees accruing to validators.

As of late June 2026, Ethereum was trading near $1,550, down from a high above $4,000 in late 2025, with the Glamsterdam timeline offering a credible fundamental catalyst for the second half of the year.
