How Ripple Won the EU When Binance Lost It: A Tale of Two MiCA Strategies

One week before Binance pulled its MiCA license application in Greece, Ripple received preliminary approval for a CASP license in Luxembourg — covering all 30 EEA countries. The contrast reveals two divergent approaches to EU crypto regulation and what they mean for the market after July 1.

On June 23, 2026, Ripple announced it had received preliminary approval for a Crypto Asset Service Provider (CASP) license from Luxembourg's Commission de Surveillance du Secteur Financier, eight days before the EU's hard July 1 deadline under the Markets in Crypto Assets regulation. The approval, structured as a formal Green Light Letter, allows Ripple to scale regulated crypto asset services across all 30 countries of the European Economic Area through a single authorization. It builds on Ripple's existing Electronic Money Institution license, obtained in Luxembourg in February 2026, giving institutional clients a complete payments infrastructure — collection, exchange, and payout — regulated under both EMI and CASP frameworks.

Ripple's Patient Strategy

Ripple's path to MiCA compliance was methodical and multi-year. The company chose Luxembourg as its European regulatory base years before MiCA took full effect, submitted full documentation to the CSSF, and built its institutional payments business in a way that mapped cleanly onto MiCA's definitions. The Luxembourg CSSF's due-diligence process focuses heavily on the 'fit and proper' character assessment of the company and its ultimate beneficial owners — a test Ripple passed without apparent complications, despite its history of protracted US SEC litigation, which was settled in 2024.

Why Binance Failed the Same Test

The contrast with Binance is instructive. Binance bet heavily on Greece as its European regulatory entry point, filing its MiCA application with the Hellenic Capital Market Commission and investing months in dialogue. On June 24, one day after Ripple's Luxembourg announcement, Binance withdrew its Greek application entirely — one week after reports emerged that the HCMC was preparing to reject it. The reported reason: the 'fit and proper' assessment of Binance's owners and managers, specifically the shadow of co-founder Changpeng Zhao's November 2023 guilty plea to US anti-money-laundering violations and his November 2024 prison sentence. MiCA's fit and proper test is designed precisely to exclude firms with this kind of regulatory history. From July 1, 2026, Binance is suspending most services for EU users — approximately 10 million accounts — including new registrations, deposits, and staking products. User funds remain withdrawable, and Binance says it will seek authorization in France and intends to return to the EU market.

What This Means for the EU Crypto Market

The divergent outcomes on the same July 1 deadline produce a competitive map that favors companies with clean regulatory histories and patient multi-year licensing strategies. Coinbase, OKX, and Crypto.com have MiCA licenses or are in advanced compliance processes. Ripple now joins them with the broadest institutional-grade authorization in the payments sector. The 10 million EU users losing Binance access represent a significant migration opportunity for licensed platforms. The deeper lesson from the Ripple-Binance contrast is that MiCA is functioning exactly as designed: as a market-structure filter that distinguishes between firms that adapted to European regulatory norms and those that tried to force a jurisdiction to overlook their regulatory track record.
