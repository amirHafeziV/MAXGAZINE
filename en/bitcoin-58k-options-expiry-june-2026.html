Bitcoin Slumps to $58K as $10.6B Quarterly Options Expiry Clears

Deribit settled its largest quarterly options expiry of 2026 on June 27, with $10.63 billion in open interest — 80% out of the money — as Bitcoin traded near $58,000. A record $692 million in ETF outflows the day before compounded the pressure.

Published MAXGAZINE Desk · 2026-06-27
AD SPACEIn-article bannerBook this spot →

The cryptocurrency market faced a double blow on June 27 as Deribit settled the largest quarterly options expiry of 2026 — a combined $10.63 billion in Bitcoin and Ethereum open interest — while Bitcoin itself was trading near $58,000, its lowest level since September 2024.

The expiry comprised approximately $9.06 billion in Bitcoin notional and $1.57 billion in Ethereum notional. Roughly 80% of total open interest had moved into out-of-the-money territory as prices fell well below the $72,000 max-pain level heading into settlement. The put-call ratio stood at 0.63 for Bitcoin and 0.50 for Ethereum, indicating that bullish call options still made up the majority of the expiring book — positions that expired worthless given the current price environment.

Record ETF outflows

The settlement coincided with the aftermath of a bruising week for Bitcoin ETF flows. On June 25, US spot Bitcoin ETFs recorded a net outflow of nearly $692 million — the largest single-day redemption since May 27, according to data tracked by major crypto analytics firms. Combined with the options expiry, more than $1.1 billion in leveraged positions were liquidated across crypto markets over the preceding 48 hours.

Macro headwinds piling up

Rising expectations for additional US Federal Reserve rate hikes have weighed heavily on risk assets. Markets were pricing an approximately 77% probability of a Fed rate increase in December as of late June, following a hawkish-toned hold at the June FOMC meeting chaired by Kevin Warsh. Bank of America is now forecasting three rate hikes in 2026, while Deutsche Bank expects two increases beginning in September.

PCE inflation data released in the days prior came in broadly in line with consensus, preventing a sharp repricing but doing little to ease the underlying macro pressure on crypto valuations.

Where analysts see the floor

Asset manager 21Shares stated in its latest semi-annual report that Bitcoin's current decline is "broadly similar" to prior post-halving pullbacks in 2016 and 2020, when corrections of 30–40% from cycle peaks preceded large subsequent rallies. The firm maintained a base-case price target of approximately $100,000 by year-end 2026. Bitcoin bears, meanwhile, are pointing to the $52,000 level as the next structural support if the current price fails to hold above $58,000.
