How the GENIUS Act Turned US Stablecoin Issuers Into Captive Buyers of Government Debt

America's first federal stablecoin law requires issuers to back tokens with cash and short-term Treasuries — channeling a fast-growing market straight into US government debt ahead of a July 18 rulemaking deadline.

The US stablecoin market is being reshaped by a single law, and the full consequences are only now coming into focus. The GENIUS Act — the first federal law in the United States to govern payment stablecoins, enacted in 2025 — requires issuers to hold reserves backing their tokens one-to-one in cash, insured bank deposits, and short-term US Treasuries. It also bars issuers from paying interest directly to token holders. Taken together, those two rules quietly turn the stablecoin industry into a structural buyer of American government debt.

A fast-growing market pointed at Treasuries

The numbers explain why this matters. Total stablecoin supply now exceeds $240 billion, with Tether's USDT holding roughly 67% of the market and Circle's USDC near 27%. To stay compliant, that collateral increasingly sits in Treasuries. Tether alone holds about $141 billion in US government debt — more than Saudi Arabia or Brazil, ranking it near the 17th-largest holder of Treasuries in the world. As stablecoin adoption grows, so does a new and reliable source of demand for short-dated US paper.

The rulemaking clock is ticking

The framework is still being built. The Treasury Department published a proposed rule defining the GENIUS Act's reserve and disclosure principles on April 1, 2026, with the public comment period closing on June 2 and further FinCEN and OFAC comment milestones in early June. Final implementing rules are due by July 18, 2026. Foreign issuers face an extra hurdle: Tether requires a Treasury reciprocity determination to keep serving US businesses, and as of May 2026 that determination had not been issued. In response, Tether launched USAT, a US-regulated token issued through Anchorage Digital Bank, while Circle has advanced its own national trust bank charter.

Why supporters and critics disagree

Supporters argue the law delivers exactly what the industry asked for: clear consumer protection, audited reserves, and the legitimacy that comes with federal oversight — plus a steady new buyer for Treasuries at a time of heavy government borrowing. Critics counter that the same design ties the crypto sector's fortunes to the Treasury market and the government's fiscal needs, and that banning yield to holders pushes savers toward tokenized money-market funds and other alternatives instead.

What to watch next

The July 18 deadline is the next real milestone, after which the practical rules of the road become clearer. Beyond that, the open questions are whether foreign issuers secure reciprocity, whether banks and large retailers launch their own stablecoins under the new regime, and how much of the next wave of stablecoin growth ultimately flows back into US government debt.
